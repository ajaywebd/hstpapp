Down Syndrome

Down syndrome is a genetic disorder. It is also called trisomy 21. It includes certain birth defects, learning problems, and facial features. A child with Down syndrome may also have heart defects and problems with vision and hearing. The severity or mildness of these problems varies from child to child.

Down syndrome is one of the most common genetic birth defects, affecting about 1 in 800 babies. Adults with Down syndrome may live about 60 years, although this can vary.

Causes

When a baby is conceived, a normal egg and sperm cell each have 46 chromosomes. These cells then divide in half, leaving 23 chromosomes each. When a sperm with 23 chromosomes fertilizes an egg with 23 chromosomes, the baby will have a full set of 46 chromosomes, half from the father and half from the mother.

Sometimes an error occurs during cell division, where an egg or sperm may keep both copies of chromosome number 21. If fertilized, the baby will have three copies of chromosome 21, known as trisomy 21.

In some cases, part of the extra chromosome 21 is attached to another chromosome, leading to translocation Down syndrome, which may be inherited from a parent. A rare form, mosaic trisomy 21, occurs when the error happens after fertilization, resulting in some cells with an extra chromosome 21.

Risk Factors

The mother’s age at childbirth is the only known factor linked to an increased risk of having a baby with Down syndrome, with the risk increasing after age 35. However, most babies with Down syndrome are born to women younger than 35 because younger women tend to have more children.

Symptoms

The symptoms of Down syndrome vary but can include:

  • Upward-slanting eyes
  • Small ears, possibly folded at the top
  • Small mouth, making the tongue appear large
  • Flattened nose bridge
  • Short neck
  • Small hands with short fingers
  • Fewer palm creases, including one across the palm and one around the base of the thumb
  • Short height
  • Loose joints

Not all children with Down syndrome will have these features. Additional health issues may include:

  • Heart defects
  • Intestinal problems
  • Vision and hearing problems
  • Thyroid problems
  • Blood conditions, such as leukemia, and increased risk of infections
  • Learning difficulties

Diagnosis

Down syndrome can often be diagnosed before birth through tests on cells from the amniotic fluid or placenta or by analyzing the baby's DNA in the mother’s blood. Fetal ultrasounds may also suggest the condition, though they are not 100% accurate.

After birth, Down syndrome may be diagnosed with a physical exam, and a blood test can confirm the presence of an extra chromosome.

Treatment

There is no cure for Down syndrome, but treatment can address specific problems such as:

  • Heart defects: About half of babies with Down syndrome have heart defects, some treatable with medication and others requiring surgery.
  • Intestinal problems: Some require surgery.
  • Vision problems: Correctable with glasses, surgery, or other treatments.
  • Hearing loss: Due to fluid in the middle ear or nerve defects, regular hearing tests are essential for early treatment.
  • Other health problems: Children with Down syndrome may experience thyroid issues, leukemia, and frequent colds or infections. Regular medical care and vaccinations are important.
  • Learning difficulties: Ranging from mild to moderate, early intervention and special education can help children reach their potential.

Some suggest high-dose vitamins improve learning and development in children with Down syndrome, but no studies have confirmed this.

Complications

Complications of Down syndrome vary depending on the severity and the body systems affected. Treatment varies accordingly, and healthcare providers will discuss appropriate options with you.

Prevention

Researchers do not know how to prevent the chromosome errors that cause Down syndrome, and there is no reason to believe a parent can cause or prevent it. However, for women who have already had a child with Down syndrome, the risk of having another child with the condition depends on various factors, including age.

Living with Down Syndrome

Children with Down syndrome can typically do most things other children can do, though they may achieve developmental milestones later. Early intervention programs are important to help them reach their potential.

Children with Down syndrome can attend school and take part in special programs to develop skills as fully as possible. Many children will learn to read, write, and participate in activities in school and their communities. Physical, occupational, and speech therapy may be necessary to assist their development.

Adults with Down syndrome can often work, live semi-independently, and participate in the community. Some may marry, but most men with Down syndrome cannot father a child, and women with Down syndrome have a 50% chance of conceiving a child with the condition, though many pregnancies are miscarried.

Key Points

  • Down syndrome (trisomy 21) is a genetic disorder involving birth defects, learning problems, and facial features. A child with Down syndrome may also have heart, vision, and hearing issues.
  • The mother’s age is the only known factor linked to the risk of Down syndrome, especially after age 35.
  • There is no cure, but treatment is available for specific symptoms and health issues.
  • Early intervention and special education programs can help children with Down syndrome reach their potential.
  • Adults with Down syndrome can often live semi-independently and hold jobs.
