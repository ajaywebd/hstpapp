Dyslexia

Dyslexia is a learning disorder that involves difficulty reading due to problems identifying speech sounds and learning how they relate to letters and words (decoding). Also called reading disability, dyslexia affects areas of the brain that process language.

People with dyslexia have normal intelligence and usually have normal vision. Most children with dyslexia can succeed in school with tutoring or a specialized education program. Emotional support also plays an important role.

Though there is no cure for dyslexia, early assessment and intervention result in the best outcome. Sometimes dyslexia goes undiagnosed for years and isn’t recognized until adulthood, but it’s never too late to seek help.

Symptoms

Signs of dyslexia can be difficult to recognize before your child enters school, but some early clues may indicate a problem. Once your child reaches school age, your child’s teacher may be the first to notice a problem. Severity varies, but the condition often becomes apparent as a child starts learning to read.

Before School

Signs that a young child may be at risk of dyslexia include:

  • Late talking
  • Learning new words slowly
  • Problems forming words correctly, such as reversing sounds in words or confusing words that sound alike
  • Problems remembering or naming letters, numbers, and colors
  • Difficulty learning nursery rhymes or playing rhyming games

School Age

Once your child is in school, dyslexia signs and symptoms may become more apparent, including:

  • Reading well below the expected level for age
  • Problems processing and understanding what he or she hears
  • Difficulty finding the right word or forming answers to questions
  • Problems remembering the sequence of things
  • Difficulty seeing (and occasionally hearing) similarities and differences in letters and words
  • Inability to sound out the pronunciation of an unfamiliar word
  • Difficulty spelling
  • Spending an unusually long time completing tasks that involve reading or writing
  • Avoiding activities that involve reading

Teens and Adults

Dyslexia signs in teens and adults are similar to those in children. Some common signs include:

  • Difficulty reading, including reading aloud
  • Slow and labor-intensive reading and writing
  • Problems spelling
  • Avoiding activities that involve reading
  • Mispronouncing names or words, or problems retrieving words
  • Trouble understanding jokes or idioms
  • Spending an unusually long time completing tasks that involve reading or writing
  • Difficulty summarizing a story
  • Trouble learning a foreign language
  • Difficulty memorizing
  • Difficulty doing math problems

Causes

Researchers haven’t yet pinpointed exactly what causes dyslexia. However, genes and brain differences play a role. Some possible causes include:

  • Genes and Heredity: Dyslexia often runs in families. About 40% of siblings of kids with dyslexia have the same reading issues, and as many as 49% of parents of kids with dyslexia have it, too. Certain genes are linked to reading and language processing problems.
  • Brain Anatomy and Activity: Brain imaging studies show differences in the brain of people with dyslexia, especially in areas related to recognizing sounds and reading. However, brain activity changes with proper tutoring due to neuroplasticity.

Risk Factors

Dyslexia risk factors include:

  • A family history of dyslexia or other learning disabilities
  • Premature birth or low birth weight
  • Exposure during pregnancy to nicotine, drugs, alcohol, or infection that may affect brain development
  • Individual differences in the parts of the brain that enable reading

Diagnosis

No single test can diagnose dyslexia. Several factors are considered, including:

  • Development, educational issues, and medical history: The doctor may ask about conditions that run in the family or learning disabilities.
  • Home life: The doctor may ask about your family environment and possible issues at home.
  • Questionnaires: Tests may be given to assess reading and language abilities.
  • Vision, hearing, and neurological tests: These tests help rule out other possible causes of reading difficulties.
  • Psychological testing: Questions to assess whether social problems, anxiety, or depression are limiting your child’s abilities.
  • Reading and academic skills tests: Your child may take educational tests, and their reading skills will be assessed by a reading expert.

Treatment

Dyslexia is a lifelong problem. However, early intervention and appropriate treatment can improve success. Treatments include:

Educational Techniques

Dyslexia is treated using specific educational techniques. Teachers may use methods that involve hearing, vision, and touch to improve reading skills, such as tracing letters while listening to sounds.

Treatment Focus

Treatment focuses on helping your child:

  • Recognize and use the smallest sounds that make up words (phonemes)
  • Understand how letters and letter combinations represent these sounds (phonics)
  • Comprehend what they are reading
  • Read aloud to build fluency
  • Build a vocabulary of recognized and understood words

Individual Education Plan (IEP)

Schools in the U.S. are legally required to provide support through an Individualized Education Plan (IEP) for children diagnosed with dyslexia.

Early Treatment

Children who receive help early (in kindergarten or first grade) can improve enough to succeed in school. Those who don’t get help until later may continue to struggle academically.

What Parents Can Do

  • Address the problem early: If you suspect dyslexia, talk to your child's doctor and pursue early intervention.
  • Read aloud to your child: Start reading to your child at a young age, and encourage listening to recorded books.
  • Work with your child’s school: Collaborate with teachers to create a plan that helps your child succeed.
  • Encourage reading time: Set aside time for regular reading practice.
  • Set an example for reading: Show your child that reading can be enjoyable by reading alongside them.

Complications

Dyslexia can lead to various complications, including trouble learning, social problems, and difficulties as an adult.

Children with dyslexia may also have attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), which can complicate treatment.
