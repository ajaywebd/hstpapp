Epilepsy

Epilepsy comes from a Greek word meaning "to hold or seize," and people who have epilepsy have seizures. You might also hear a seizure called a convulsion, fit, or spell.

Your brain cells are constantly sending out electrical signals that travel along nerves to the rest of the body. These signals tell the muscles to move so you can do your normal activities. During a seizure, a person's muscles tighten and relax rapidly or stop moving completely. Seizures come on suddenly, and people who have them cannot control their muscles while they are having a seizure.

Depending on where in the brain a seizure is happening, a person's behavior may change in different ways. If too many brain cells are sending signals at the same time, it causes an overload, and a person may pass out and shake all over. People who have epilepsy may have seizures only once in a while or as frequently as every day.

What's a Seizure?

A seizure is a change in a person’s behavior that comes from abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Most seizures occur without warning, although some people have a funny feeling, an upset stomach, or a weird smell or taste right before a seizure. This is called an aura. Others find that certain things may bring on a seizure, like not getting enough sleep or playing video games.

During a seizure, the person may fall down, shake, stiffen, throw up, drool, urinate (pee), or lose bowel control. Other seizures seem less dramatic. Someone might just stare into space or have jerking movements in one part of the body. When the seizure is over, the person may feel sleepy and won’t remember what happened.

Who has epilepsy?

About 3 million Americans have epilepsy, including boys and girls and people of all races and ages. Seizures can start at any age, but often they begin before age 10 or after age 55.

Doctors often can’t explain why a person has epilepsy. They do know that epilepsy is not contagious — you can’t catch it from somebody. Epilepsy is not passed down through families (inherited) in the same way that blue eyes or brown hair are. But if somebody’s mom or dad or sibling has epilepsy, then he or she has a slightly higher risk for epilepsy than somebody whose family has no history of seizures.

Causes of epilepsy

Some children develop epilepsy as a result of brain injury, which could be due to a severe head injury, complications during birth, or infections like meningitis that affect the brain. Epilepsy caused by such structural changes is called symptomatic epilepsy.

Researchers also believe that the development of epilepsy is often linked to genetics. A person's genetic tendency combined with factors like brain injury can contribute to the onset of epilepsy, though not everyone with similar injuries develops epilepsy.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of seizures include:

  • Staring and unresponsiveness
  • Confusion
  • Jerks and twitches
  • Wandering
  • Shaking or falling
  • Picking or lip-smacking

First aid for seizures

For non-convulsive seizures:

  • Watch the person carefully to recognize the seizure
  • Speak quietly and calmly
  • Explain to others what is happening
  • Guide the person to a safe area
  • Don't restrain or stop movements
  • Stay with the person until full consciousness is regained

For convulsive (grand mal) seizures:

  • Time the seizure
  • Look for medical identification, such as a seizure bracelet
  • Turn the person on their side, away from dangerous objects
  • Do not put anything in the person's mouth
  • Remove tight clothing or objects around the neck
  • Call 9-1-1 if the seizure lasts more than five minutes

Diagnosis

A complete physical and neurological examination is conducted to understand the cause of seizures. An Electroencephalogram (EEG) records electrical activity in the brain and provides insights into the location and type of epileptic discharges.

Additional tests may include:

  • Brain imaging studies such as MRI, PET, or SPECT
  • Neuropsychological testing to assess cognitive functions affected by epilepsy
  • Blood and urine tests to rule out other underlying conditions

Treatment

The general practitioner (GP) will often refer a child with epilepsy to a pediatrician or pediatric neurologist for further care. From around age 16, care may be transferred to an adult neurologist.

Anti-epileptic drugs (AEDs)

Most people with epilepsy are treated with AEDs, which aim to prevent seizures but cannot stop seizures that are already occurring. AEDs are generally effective but may cause side effects.

Other treatments

If AEDs do not control seizures, other treatments may be considered:

  • Ketogenic diet: A high-fat, low-carbohydrate diet that may reduce seizures in children with medication-resistant epilepsy.
  • Epilepsy surgery: Surgery may be an option for some children, depending on the type of epilepsy and the part of the brain affected.
