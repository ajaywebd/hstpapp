Ebola

Ebola is a serious and deadly virus transmitted by animals and humans. It was initially detected in 1976 in Sudan and the Democratic Republic of Congo. Researchers named the disease after the Ebola River. Until recently, Ebola appeared only in Africa.

Although the Ebola virus has been present for more than 35 years, the largest outbreak began in West Africa in March 2014. This outbreak has proven more deadly, severe, and widespread than previous outbreaks. While cases have significantly declined since the peak of the outbreak, there's still a chance of further outbreaks. Learning the facts about the virus can help prevent the spread of this deadly infection.

Causes

The Ebola virus belongs to the viral family Filoviridae, also known as Filovirus. These viruses cause hemorrhagic fever or profuse bleeding inside and outside the body, accompanied by a high fever. The Ebola virus likely originated in African fruit bats. It is classified as a zoonotic virus because it is transmitted to humans from animals. Humans can also transfer the virus to one another. The following animals can transmit the virus:

  • Chimpanzees
  • Forest antelopes
  • Gorillas
  • Monkeys
  • Porcupines

People may handle infected animals, which can transmit the virus via blood and body fluids.

Risk Factors

Unlike some other viruses, Ebola cannot be transmitted through the air or by touch alone. Direct contact with bodily fluids from an infected person is required for transmission. Ebola can be spread through:

  • Blood
  • Diarrhea
  • Breast milk
  • Feces
  • Saliva
  • Semen
  • Sweat
  • Urine
  • Vomit

These bodily fluids can carry the Ebola virus, and transmission can occur via the eyes, nose, mouth, broken skin, or sexual contact. Healthcare workers are especially at risk due to their exposure to infected blood and fluids.

Other risk factors include:

  • Exposure to infected objects, such as needles
  • Interactions with infected animals
  • Attending burial ceremonies of someone who died from Ebola
  • Traveling to areas where a recent outbreak has occurred

Symptoms

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), Ebola symptoms typically appear within 8 to 10 days after exposure, though they can appear as early as 2 days or as late as 3 weeks.

Extreme fatigue is often the first prominent symptom. Other symptoms include:

  • Diarrhea
  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Muscle pain
  • Stomach pain
  • Unexplained bleeding or bruising
  • Vomiting

If you have come in contact with someone diagnosed with Ebola or handled infected animals and have any of these symptoms, you should seek immediate medical attention.

Diagnosis

The early symptoms of Ebola can closely mimic other diseases such as the flu, malaria, and typhoid fever. Blood tests can identify antibodies of the Ebola virus, along with other indicators such as:

  • Low or high white blood cell counts
  • Low platelet counts
  • Elevated liver enzymes
  • Abnormal coagulation factor levels

If no symptoms appear within 21 days after exposure, Ebola is ruled out.

Treatment

There is no specific cure or vaccine for Ebola at this time. Supportive care is provided to manage symptoms, including:

  • Giving medications to maintain blood pressure
  • Managing electrolyte balances
  • Providing extra oxygen, if needed
  • Providing intravenous or oral fluids to prevent dehydration
  • Treating coexisting infections
  • Administering blood products if indicated

Prevention

Preventive measures include:

  • Avoiding contact with blood and body fluids
  • Practicing careful hand hygiene with soap and water or alcohol-based hand sanitizers
  • Refraining from handling items a person with Ebola has touched, including clothing, bedding, needles, or medical equipment
  • Wearing protective clothing when in contact with wildlife or infected individuals

Healthcare workers and lab technicians must take precautions such as wearing protective gowns, gloves, masks, and eye shields when handling Ebola patients or their belongings.

Complications

Some people recover from Ebola without complications, but others may experience lingering effects, including:

  • Joint problems
  • Hair loss
  • Fatigue
  • Delirium
  • Inflammation of the liver and eyes
  • Sensory changes
  • Jaundice

Severe complications include multiple organ failure, coma, shock, and severe bleeding.

Outlook

According to the WHO, the average fatality rate for Ebola is 50%, though some virus strains are deadlier than others. The earlier the infection is diagnosed, the better the outlook for recovery.

The CDC estimates that Ebola survivors have antibodies to the virus for about 10 years. Until a vaccine is available, it is important to remain cautious to avoid the spread of the virus.
