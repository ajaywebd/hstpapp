Congenital heart defects

If your child has a congenital heart defect, it means that your child was born with a problem in the structure of his or her heart.

Some congenital heart defects in children are simple and don't need treatment. Other congenital heart defects in children are more complex and may require several surgeries performed over a period of several years.

Learning about your child's congenital heart defect can help you understand the condition and know what you can expect in the coming months and years.

Symptoms

Serious congenital heart defects usually become evident soon after birth or during the first few months of life. Signs and symptoms could include:

  • Pale gray or blue skin color (cyanosis)
  • Rapid breathing
  • Swelling in the legs, abdomen, or areas around the eyes
  • Shortness of breath during feedings, leading to poor weight gain

Less serious congenital heart defects may not be diagnosed until later in childhood. If signs and symptoms are evident in older children, they may include:

  • Easily becoming short of breath during exercise or activity
  • Easily tiring during exercise or activity
  • Fainting during exercise or activity
  • Swelling in the hands, ankles, or feet

How the heart works

The heart is divided into four hollow chambers, two on the right and two on the left. To pump blood throughout the body, the heart uses its left and right sides for different tasks.

The right side of the heart moves blood to the lungs through vessels called pulmonary arteries. In the lungs, blood picks up oxygen then returns to the heart's left side through the pulmonary veins. The left side of the heart then pumps the blood through the aorta and out to the rest of the body.

How heart defects develop

During the first six weeks of pregnancy, the heart begins taking shape and starts beating. The major blood vessels that run to and from the heart also begin to form during this critical time during gestation.

It's at this point in your baby's development that heart defects may begin to develop. Researchers aren't sure exactly what causes most of these defects, but they think genetics, certain medical conditions, some medications, and environmental factors, such as smoking, may play a role.

Types of heart defects

There are many different types of congenital heart defects, falling mainly into these categories:

  • Holes in the heart. These can form in the walls between major blood vessels leaving the heart. A ventricular septal defect is a hole in the wall between the right and left ventricles. An atrial septal defect occurs when there's a hole between the atria.
  • Obstructed blood flow. When blood vessels or heart valves are narrow, the heart must work harder to pump blood, leading to heart enlargement.
  • Abnormal blood vessels. Congenital heart defects can occur when blood vessels going to and from the heart don't form correctly or are not positioned properly.
  • Heart valve abnormalities. If the heart valves can't open and close correctly, blood flow is restricted.
  • An underdeveloped heart. In conditions like hypoplastic left heart syndrome, the left side of the heart hasn't developed enough to pump blood effectively.
  • A combination of defects. Tetralogy of Fallot is a combination of four defects: a hole between ventricles, a narrowed passage to the pulmonary artery, a shift in the connection of the aorta, and thickened right ventricular muscle.

Risk factors

  • Rubella (German measles). Having rubella during pregnancy can cause problems in your baby's heart development.
  • Diabetes. Controlling your diabetes before and during pregnancy can reduce the risk of congenital heart defects.
  • Medications. Certain medications taken during pregnancy can increase the risk of congenital heart defects.
  • Drinking alcohol during pregnancy. Avoid alcohol during pregnancy as it increases the risk of heart defects.
  • Smoking. Smoking during pregnancy increases the likelihood of congenital heart defects.
  • Heredity. Congenital heart defects sometimes run in families and may be associated with genetic syndromes.

Diagnosis

If it's possible your child has a heart defect, your doctor may order several tests, including:

  • Fetal echocardiogram. This test allows your doctor to see if your child has a heart defect before he or she is born, using an ultrasound to create a picture of the baby's heart.
  • Echocardiogram. This test, performed after birth, uses ultrasound to produce images of the heart, allowing the doctor to see any abnormalities in the heart muscle and valves.
  • Electrocardiogram. This test records the electrical activity of your child’s heart and helps diagnose rhythm problems or other defects.
  • Chest X-ray. A chest X-ray can show if the heart is enlarged or if there is extra blood or fluid in the lungs.
  • Pulse oximetry. This non-invasive test measures how much oxygen is in your child’s blood using a sensor placed over the child’s finger.
  • Cardiac catheterization. A thin, flexible tube is inserted into a blood vessel and guided to the heart. This test gives detailed information about the heart defect and sometimes allows for treatment during the procedure.
  • Cardiovascular magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). MRI technology provides a detailed image of the heart and is increasingly used to evaluate congenital heart defects in adolescents and adults.

Treatment

A congenital heart defect may not require long-term treatment, and in some instances, defects can correct themselves as the child grows. However, some defects are serious and require immediate treatment. The treatments include:

  • Procedures using catheterization. Some defects can be repaired using catheterization techniques, avoiding open-heart surgery. A catheter is inserted into a vein and guided to the heart where tools can repair the defect.
  • Open-heart surgery. Some defects require surgery to repair the heart. In some cases, minimally invasive heart surgery is an option.
  • Heart transplant. In severe cases, a heart transplant may be necessary if the defect cannot be repaired.
  • Medications. Medications can help the heart work more efficiently and treat symptoms such as high blood pressure and fluid retention. Some medications are prescribed to manage irregular heartbeats (arrhythmias).

Long-term treatment

Some children with congenital heart defects require multiple procedures and surgeries throughout their lives. Lifelong monitoring by cardiologists is necessary even after corrective surgery. As the child grows older, care will transition to an adult congenital cardiologist, as congenital heart defects can affect adult life.

Exercise restrictions

Although some children may need to limit physical activity, many can participate in normal or near-normal activities. Parents should work with doctors to determine which activities are safe for their child.

Infection prevention

Depending on the type of congenital heart defect and the surgery used to correct it, your child may need to take extra steps to prevent infections, such as taking antibiotics before certain medical or dental procedures to prevent infective endocarditis.

Complications

  • Congestive heart failure. This can develop in babies with significant heart defects. Signs include rapid breathing and poor weight gain.
  • Slower growth and development. Children with serious heart defects may develop and grow more slowly than other children.
  • Heart rhythm problems. Arrhythmias can be caused by congenital heart defects or scarring after surgery.
  • Cyanosis. Some children with heart defects may develop a grayish-blue skin color due to oxygen-poor blood mixing with oxygen-rich blood.
  • Stroke. Some children with congenital heart defects are at higher risk of stroke due to blood clots traveling through a hole in the heart to the brain.
  • Emotional issues. Children with congenital heart defects may feel insecure or develop emotional problems due to activity restrictions or learning difficulties.

Prevention

While it’s not always possible to prevent congenital heart defects, there are things you can do to reduce the risk:

  • Get a rubella (German measles) vaccine. Be vaccinated before trying to conceive.
  • Control chronic medical conditions. Manage diabetes and other chronic conditions to reduce the risk of heart defects.
  • Avoid harmful substances. Avoid exposure to harmful substances like alcohol, tobacco, and certain medications during pregnancy.
  • Take a multivitamin with folic acid. Daily consumption of 400 micrograms of folic acid has been shown to reduce the risk of birth defects, including heart defects.
