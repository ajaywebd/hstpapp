Epilepsy

Epilepsy originates from a Greek word meaning "to hold or seize." It refers to a neurological condition where individuals experience seizures, also known as convulsions, fits, or spells. Seizures occur due to abnormal electrical activity in the brain, causing uncontrolled muscle movements or other behavioral changes.

What's a Seizure?

A seizure is a sudden change in behavior resulting from abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Seizures vary widely in symptoms, which may include:

  • Falling down and shaking
  • Stiffening
  • Vomiting or drooling
  • Urination or loss of bowel control
  • Staring into space or jerking movements in one part of the body

Some people experience an aura before a seizure, marked by unusual feelings, smells, or tastes.

Who Has Epilepsy?

Approximately 3 million Americans, including individuals of all ages and races, have epilepsy. Seizures can begin at any age, often before age 10 or after age 55. While epilepsy is not contagious, people with a family history of epilepsy may have a slightly higher risk.

Causes of Epilepsy

The causes of epilepsy include:

  • Severe head injuries
  • Complications during birth
  • Brain infections like meningitis
  • Structural changes in the brain

Genetic predisposition often plays a role, interacting with structural brain changes to increase the likelihood of seizures.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of epilepsy and seizures include:

  • Staring and unresponsiveness
  • Confusion
  • Jerks and twitches
  • Wandering
  • Shaking or falling
  • Picking or lip-smacking behaviors

First Aid for Seizures :-

For Non-Convulsive Seizures:

  • Watch the person carefully to recognize the seizure.
  • Speak quietly and calmly to the person.
  • Explain to others what is happening.
  • Guide the person gently to a safe area away from danger, such as water, machinery, or fire.
  • Don't restrain the person or try to stop their movements.
  • Stay with the person until they regain full consciousness.

For Convulsive (Grand Mal) Seizures:

  • Time the seizure.
  • Look for an "epilepsy" or "seizure disorders" bracelet.
  • Place the person on their side, away from hazardous objects.
  • Do not put anything in the person’s mouth.
  • Remove eyeglasses and loosen tight objects around the neck.
  • Call emergency services (e.g., 9-1-1) if the seizure lasts more than five minutes or causes injury.
  • Stay with the person until help arrives.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing epilepsy involves a complete physical and neurological examination. Key steps include:

  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): Records electrical activity in the brain to identify epileptic discharges.
  • Imaging Studies: High-resolution MRI and other scans such as PET, SPECT, or fMRI to detect abnormalities in the brain.
  • Neuropsychological Testing: Evaluates the impact of epilepsy on cognitive and emotional functions, including memory and attention.
  • Blood and Urine Tests: Analyzes blood cell counts, sugar, electrolytes, and other factors to rule out metabolic or genetic diseases.

Treatment Options :

Anti-Epileptic Drugs (AEDs):

Most children with epilepsy take AEDs to control seizures. While AEDs are effective for many, they do not cure epilepsy or stop seizures in progress. Side effects are possible but may subside as the body adjusts. Consult a healthcare provider before making any changes to medication.

Ketogenic Diet:

For children who don’t respond to AEDs, the ketogenic diet, supervised by specialists and dietitians, may reduce the frequency or severity of seizures.

Epilepsy Surgery:

In specific cases, surgery to remove the part of the brain causing seizures may be an option. The decision depends on the type and location of epilepsy in the brain.
