Fast Facts on Albinism

  • There is no cure for albinism, but some symptoms can be treated.
  • Albinism is a genetic disorder.
  • Primarily, albinism affects the hair, eyes, skin, and vision.
  • The most common cause of albinism is an interruption in the functioning of the enzyme tyrosinase.
  • An estimated 1 in 70 people carry the genes associated with albinism.

What is Albinism?

Albinism is an inherited disease characterized by a substantially lower rate of melanin production. Melanin is the pigment responsible for the color of the skin, hair, and eyes.

People with albinism often have lighter-colored skin and hair compared to their family or ethnic group, but this is not always the case. Vision is always affected to some degree. Due to reduced melanin levels, individuals with albinism are more sensitive to UV exposure and have an increased risk of skin cancer.

Symptoms

The primary symptoms of albinism can be categorized as follows:

Skin:

People with albinism typically have lighter skin tones, but skin tone may darken with age as melanin levels increase. Some individuals may develop freckles, pink moles, or large freckle-like spots called lentigines after sun exposure.

Hair:

Hair color ranges from white to brown, with variations like yellow or reddish hues in people of African or Asian descent. Hair may darken with age.

Eye Color:

Eye color varies from light blue to brown and can change with age. Low melanin levels can make the eyes appear translucent, and in certain lighting, they may look red or pink. This makes individuals sensitive to light (photosensitive).

Vision:

Vision is always affected by albinism, with possible changes including:

  • Nystagmus: Rapid, uncontrollable eye movement.
  • Strabismus: Eyes not functioning in unison.
  • Amblyopia: Also known as "lazy eye."
  • Nearsightedness or Farsightedness: Extreme cases are common.
  • Photophobia: Sensitivity to light.
  • Optic Nerve Hypoplasia: Underdeveloped optic nerve.
  • Optic Nerve Misrouting: Unusual nerve routes from retina to brain.
  • Astigmatism: Blurred vision due to abnormal curvature of the eye or lens.

Vision issues are most severe in newborns but generally improve within the first six months of life. However, they rarely achieve normal levels of eye health.

Causes

Albinism is caused by a mutation in one of several genes responsible for various aspects of melanin production by melanocytes in the skin and eyes. Most commonly, these mutations interfere with the enzyme tyrosinase (tyrosine 3-monooxygenase), which synthesizes melanin from the amino acid tyrosine.

  • Melanin production can be slowed or completely stopped depending on the mutation.
  • Associated problems with the visual system are always present because melanin plays a vital role in the development of the retina and the optic nerve pathways from the eye to the brain.

Types

Albinism is classified into subgroups based on the specific genes affected. These include:

  • X-linked ocular albinism: Caused by a mutation in the X chromosome, it primarily affects males. Vision problems are present, but hair, skin, and eye color are generally normal.
  • Hermansky-Pudlak syndrome: A rare variant most common in Puerto Rico. Symptoms resemble oculocutaneous albinism but are accompanied by a higher risk of bowel, heart, kidney, lung diseases, and bleeding disorders.
  • Chediak-Higashi syndrome: A rare form caused by mutations in the CHS1/LYST genes. Symptoms include silvery hair, slightly gray skin, and increased susceptibility to infections due to white blood cell defects.
  • Oculocutaneous albinism (OCA): This type is caused by mutations in one of four genes and has seven subtypes:
    • OCA type 1: Individuals typically have milky skin, white hair, and blue eyes. Skin and hair may darken with age.
    • OCA type 2: Similar to type 1 but occurs more often in sub-Saharan Africans, African-Americans, and Native Americans.
    • OCA type 3: Primarily affects black South Africans.
    • OCA type 4: Most common in East Asian populations.

As research progresses, more specific mutations are being identified, furthering our understanding of albinism.

Inheritance

Albinism is inherited through two primary patterns: autosomal recessive and X-linked inheritance.

Autosomal Recessive Inheritance

In autosomal recessive inheritance, a person must inherit faulty copies of a gene from both parents to develop albinism.

  • If both parents carry the gene, there is a 1 in 4 chance their child will have albinism and a 1 in 2 chance the child will be a carrier without symptoms.
  • Approximately 1 in 70 people carry the gene for albinism but do not show symptoms.

X-Linked Inheritance

X-linked recessive conditions primarily affect males, as they have only one X chromosome. If this chromosome carries a mutation, the condition will develop.

  • Females, with two X chromosomes, can carry the mutation without developing the condition, as the healthy X chromosome compensates.
  • If the mother has an X-linked mutation, each daughter has a 1 in 2 chance of being a carrier, and each son has a 1 in 2 chance of having albinism.

Diagnosis

The process of diagnosing albinism most often involves:

  • Physical examination.
  • Discussion about skin and hair pigmentation changes.
  • Examination of the eyes by an ophthalmologist (eye specialist).
  • Comparing the individual's coloration to family members.

Other diseases can also cause changes in pigmentation, but they do not cause changes in vision. If both pigment and vision changes are present, albinism is the most likely cause.

The most reliable way to diagnose albinism is through genetic testing. However, if there is a family history of albinism, genetic testing may not be necessary.

Treatment

Since albinism is a genetic condition, there are no cures. Treatment focuses on managing symptoms and monitoring for any changes.

  • Proper eye care, including prescription glasses, dark glasses to protect against the sun, and regular eye exams.
  • Skin monitoring and protection using sunblock to prevent sunburn and skin damage.
  • Surgery on the optical muscles to reduce "shaking" in nystagmus, or on strabismus to improve eye alignment. However, these surgeries do not improve vision, and the outcomes vary.

Albinism does not worsen with age. Children with albinism can lead successful lives, achieving the same educational and employment opportunities as others.

Complications :

Physical Complications

The most common physical problems associated with albinism are:

  • Increased risk of sunburn.
  • Higher susceptibility to skin cancers.

Social Complications

People with albinism may face significant social challenges:

  • Bullying at school or feeling isolated due to looking different from family or peers.
  • Stress, low self-esteem, and social isolation.

In sub-Saharan Africa, individuals with albinism face even greater difficulties. A study in Nigeria revealed that affected individuals experienced alienation, avoided social interactions, and were less likely to complete schooling, find employment, or form partnerships.

Worse still, in some African countries, such as Tanzania and Burundi, individuals with albinism face violence. Their body parts are sought after by witch doctors for profit. For example, in 2013, National Geographic estimated that the body parts of an individual with albinism could be sold for up to $75,000.

In Tanzania and Zimbabwe, a false belief persists that having sex with a woman with albinism cures AIDS, leading to rape, murder, and further spread of the disease.
