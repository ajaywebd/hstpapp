Cleft Lip and Cleft Palate

Cleft lip and cleft palate are birth abnormalities of the mouth and lip. In the United States, nearly 6,800 babies are born with oral clefts annually. These abnormalities occur less frequently among African-Americans. They arise early in pregnancy when the sides of the lip and the roof of the mouth do not fuse as they should. A child can have cleft lip, cleft palate, or both, with boys more commonly affected. Most babies born with a cleft are otherwise healthy with no additional abnormalities.

Cleft Palate

Cleft palate occurs when the roof of the mouth does not completely close, leaving an opening that may extend into the nasal cavity. It can involve either side of the palate, extending from the front of the mouth (hard palate) to the throat (soft palate). Often, it includes the lip as well. Cleft palate is less noticeable than cleft lip because it is located inside the mouth. In some cases, cleft palate runs in families.

Cleft Lip

Cleft lip occurs when the lip does not fully form during fetal development. It can range from mild (a small notch in the lip) to severe (a large opening extending to the nose). Cleft lip is categorized based on location and severity:

  • Unilateral incomplete: A cleft on one side of the lip that does not extend to the nose.
  • Unilateral complete: A cleft on one side of the lip extending into the nose.
  • Bilateral complete: A cleft involving both sides of the lip and the nose.

Causes

The exact cause of cleft lip and palate is not fully understood. They result from a combination of genetic and environmental factors, described as "multifactorial inheritance." The risk of recurrence in a family depends on how many relatives have clefts. If both parents are unaffected but have one child with a cleft, the chance of having another affected child is 3-5%. Genetic consultation is recommended for families with a history of clefts.

Complications

  • Feeding difficulties: Infants with cleft palate may struggle to suck properly due to the incomplete roof of the mouth.
  • Ear infections and hearing loss: Dysfunction of the tube connecting the middle ear and throat can lead to recurrent infections and potential hearing loss.
  • Speech and language delays: Muscle function may be reduced, causing delays or abnormalities in speech. Referral to a speech therapist is often recommended.
  • Dental problems: Teeth may erupt abnormally, often requiring orthodontic care.

Feeding My Baby with Cleft Palate

The most immediate concern for a baby with cleft palate is good nutrition. Sucking for children with a cleft palate is difficult because of the poorly formed roof of the mouth. Children with just a cleft lip (without a cleft palate) do not routinely have feeding difficulties. Below are some suggestions to help feed your infant:

  • Breastfeeding may take extra time and patience. If it's not providing adequate nutrition, consider alternatives like pumping breast milk and feeding through other techniques.
  • Hold your infant in an upright position to prevent food from coming out of the nose.
  • Consult your child’s doctor about suitable feeding devices for your baby.
  • Offer small, frequent feedings to ensure your infant gets enough calories to gain weight.

Treatment for Cleft Lip and Cleft Palate

Cleft lip and/or palate is typically corrected with surgery, though children may require additional services like dental or orthodontic care before surgery. The Cleft and Craniofacial team will work closely with you and your pediatrician to determine the best treatment plan for your child. Key treatment options include:

  • Nasal Alveolar Molding: A technique used before surgery to shape the gums, lip, and nostrils.
  • Surgery for Cleft Lip: Performed within the first 3 to 6 months of life, depending on the surgeon’s recommendations, to repair the separation of the lip.
  • Surgery for Cleft Palate: Typically done between 10 to 12 months of age to fix the roof of the mouth, enabling normal eating and speech development.

Living with Cleft Lip or Cleft Palate

Children with cleft lip or palate can live healthy, happy lives. While some may face challenges such as teasing or bullying, they can find support from parents, teachers, counselors, and their cleft palate team. Kids should be encouraged to embrace their unique qualities and seek help when needed to navigate social situations. With proper treatment and care, they can grow into confident, well-adjusted adults.
