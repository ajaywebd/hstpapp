Common Cold

The common cold is a viral infection of your nose and throat (upper respiratory tract). It's usually harmless, although it might not feel that way. Many types of viruses can cause a common cold. Children younger than 6 are at greatest risk of colds, but healthy adults can also expect to have two or three colds annually. Most people recover from a common cold in a week or 10 days. Symptoms might last longer in people who smoke. If symptoms don't improve, see your doctor.

Symptoms

Symptoms of a common cold typically appear one to three days after exposure to the virus. They can vary but often include:

  • Runny or stuffy nose
  • Sore throat
  • Cough
  • Congestion
  • Slight body aches or mild headache
  • Sneezing
  • Low-grade fever
  • Generally feeling unwell (malaise)

Nasal discharge may become thicker and change color as the cold progresses, which is not necessarily a sign of a bacterial infection.

When to See a Doctor

While most colds resolve on their own, seek medical attention if your child experiences:

  • Fever of 100.4 F (38 C) in newborns up to 12 weeks
  • Rising fever or fever lasting more than two days
  • Worsening or non-improving symptoms
  • Severe symptoms, such as intense headache or persistent cough
  • Wheezing
  • Ear pain
  • Extreme fussiness or unusual drowsiness
  • Lack of appetite

Causes

The most common cause of the common cold is rhinoviruses. A cold virus typically enters the body through the mouth, eyes, or nose and spreads via:

  • Droplets from coughing, sneezing, or talking
  • Hand-to-hand contact with an infected person
  • Sharing contaminated objects like utensils, toys, or telephones

Risk Factors

Factors that increase the risk of catching a cold include:

  • Age (children under 6 are at higher risk)
  • Weakened immune system
  • Time of year (fall and winter)
  • Exposure to cigarette smoke
  • Being in crowded places, like schools or airplanes

Complications

Although usually mild, a cold can lead to complications such as:

  • Acute ear infection (otitis media): Symptoms include ear pain or green/yellow nasal discharge.
  • Asthma: Colds can trigger asthma attacks.
  • Acute sinusitis: Persistent colds may cause sinus inflammation or infection.
  • Secondary infections: Strep throat, pneumonia, or croup may develop and require medical treatment.

Prevention

While there's no vaccine for the common cold, you can take precautions to reduce the risk of catching or spreading cold viruses:

  • Wash your hands frequently and thoroughly with soap and water. Use alcohol-based hand sanitizer if soap isn't available.
  • Disinfect frequently touched surfaces like kitchen and bathroom counters.
  • Use tissues when sneezing or coughing, dispose of them promptly, and wash your hands.
  • Teach children to sneeze or cough into the bend of their elbow if no tissue is available.
  • Avoid sharing utensils, glasses, or towels, and use labeled items for sick individuals.
  • Stay away from close contact with people who have colds.
  • Choose child care centers with strict hygiene policies and rules about sick children staying home.
  • Maintain a healthy lifestyle, including good nutrition, exercise, sufficient sleep, and stress management.

Diagnosis

Common colds are usually diagnosed based on symptoms. If a bacterial infection or other condition is suspected, your doctor may order tests like a chest X-ray.

Treatment

There's no cure for the common cold. Treatment focuses on symptom relief. Antibiotics are ineffective against viruses and should only be used if a bacterial infection is present.

  • Pain relievers: Acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen can reduce fever, sore throat, and headaches. Avoid aspirin for children or teens recovering from flu-like symptoms due to Reye's syndrome risk.
  • Decongestant sprays: Adults can use nasal sprays for up to five days. They are not recommended for children under 6.
  • Cough syrups: Over-the-counter cough medicines are not recommended for children under 4. For older children, follow label directions carefully to avoid overdose.

Lifestyle and Home Remedies

To ease symptoms and improve comfort during a cold:

  • Drink plenty of fluids like water, juice, or clear broth to stay hydrated. Avoid caffeine and alcohol.
  • Eat chicken soup or warm fluids to soothe congestion.
  • Rest and stay home if you're unwell to recover and prevent spreading the virus.
  • Adjust room temperature and humidity. Use a cool-mist humidifier to moisten dry air and ease congestion.
  • Soothe your throat with a saltwater gargle (1/4 to 1/2 teaspoon of salt in 4 to 8 ounces of warm water).
  • Use saline nasal drops to relieve congestion. For infants, suction nostrils gently with a bulb syringe after applying saline drops.
