Congenital Heart Defects

If your child has a congenital heart defect, it means they were born with a structural problem in their heart. Some defects are minor and don't require treatment, while others may need multiple surgeries over several years. Understanding your child's condition can help you prepare for the future.

Symptoms

Serious congenital heart defects often appear shortly after birth or in early infancy. Symptoms include:

  • Pale gray or blue skin color (cyanosis)
  • Rapid breathing
  • Swelling in the legs, abdomen, or around the eyes
  • Shortness of breath during feedings, leading to poor weight gain

In older children, symptoms may include:

  • Shortness of breath during exercise or activity
  • Fatigue during exercise or activity
  • Fainting during exercise
  • Swelling in the hands, ankles, or feet

Causes

Congenital heart defects develop during the first six weeks of pregnancy when the heart begins to form. While the exact cause is often unknown, a combination of genetic and environmental factors may contribute.

Types of Heart Defects

Congenital heart defects are classified into the following categories:

  • Holes in the heart: These allow oxygen-poor blood to mix with oxygen-rich blood. Examples include atrial septal defects, ventricular septal defects, and patent ductus arteriosus.
  • Obstructed blood flow: Narrowed valves or vessels cause the heart to work harder. Examples include pulmonary stenosis and aortic stenosis.
  • Abnormal blood vessels: Improperly formed or positioned vessels, such as in transposition of the great arteries or coarctation of the aorta.
  • Heart valve abnormalities: Faulty valves prevent smooth blood flow, as seen in Ebstein's anomaly and pulmonary atresia.
  • Underdeveloped heart: Parts of the heart, like in hypoplastic left heart syndrome, don't fully develop.
  • Combination of defects: Some children are born with multiple defects, such as in Tetralogy of Fallot.

Risk Factors

Several environmental and genetic factors may increase the risk of congenital heart defects:

  • Rubella (German measles): Contracting rubella during pregnancy can affect heart development.
  • Diabetes: Controlling diabetes before and during pregnancy reduces risk.
  • Medications: Certain drugs, including ACE inhibitors and isotretinoin, may increase the risk.
  • Alcohol and smoking: Both are linked to a higher likelihood of congenital heart defects.
  • Heredity: Genetic syndromes like Down syndrome or deletions on chromosome 22 are associated with heart defects.

Diagnosis

If a congenital heart defect is suspected, various diagnostic tools may be used:

  • Fetal echocardiogram: An ultrasound before birth to detect defects.
  • Echocardiogram: Produces images of the heart to assess abnormalities.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG): Measures electrical activity of the heart.
  • Chest X-ray: Identifies enlargement of the heart or fluid in the lungs.
  • Pulse oximetry: Measures oxygen levels in the blood.
  • Cardiac catheterization: Provides detailed images and allows certain treatments.
  • Cardiovascular MRI: Offers high-resolution images for adolescents and adults.

Treatment

Some congenital heart defects resolve on their own, while others require medical intervention. Treatment options include:

  • Catheterization procedures: Minimally invasive techniques to repair defects without opening the chest.
  • Open-heart surgery: Used for more complex defects, requiring chest incisions to access and repair the heart.
  • Heart transplant: For severe defects that cannot be repaired surgically.
  • Medications: Drugs such as ACE inhibitors, beta blockers, and diuretics help manage symptoms and ease heart stress.

Some treatments may need to be performed in stages over several years, and others might require follow-up procedures as the child grows.

Long-Term Treatment for Congenital Heart Defects

Children with congenital heart defects may require multiple surgeries and lifelong care. While many can lead normal lives post-treatment, ongoing monitoring and adjustments are often necessary.

Lifelong Monitoring and Treatment

  • After corrective surgery, children need regular follow-ups with a pediatric cardiologist. As they age, care transitions to an adult congenital cardiologist to manage potential health issues that may arise over time.
  • Encourage your child to stay informed about their condition and share their medical history with healthcare providers throughout life.

Exercise Restrictions

Many children with congenital heart defects can engage in normal activities, but some may need to limit exercise. Your child's doctor will provide guidance on safe activities.

Infection Prevention

  • Some heart defects increase the risk of infections, such as infective endocarditis. Antibiotics may be required before surgeries or dental procedures.
  • Children with prosthetic devices or materials in their heart are at a higher risk of infection. Consult your child's cardiologist for preventive measures.

Complications

  • Congestive heart failure: Rapid breathing and poor weight gain may indicate heart failure in babies.
  • Slower growth and development: Children with severe defects may experience delayed growth and milestones.
  • Heart rhythm problems: Arrhythmias can result from the defect or scar tissue after surgery.
  • Cyanosis: A grayish-blue skin tone caused by mixing oxygen-poor and oxygen-rich blood.
  • Stroke: Blood clots traveling through heart defects may increase the risk of stroke.
  • Emotional issues: Physical restrictions or learning challenges can lead to emotional insecurities. Seek professional support if needed.
  • Lifelong follow-up: Regular cardiologist visits are essential to monitor risks like endocarditis, heart failure, or valve issues.

Prevention

  • Vaccination: Get vaccinated for rubella before pregnancy to prevent heart development issues.
  • Manage chronic conditions: Properly control diabetes or epilepsy to reduce risks during pregnancy.
  • Avoid harmful substances: Steer clear of alcohol, smoking, and strong chemicals during pregnancy.
  • Take folic acid: A daily multivitamin with 400 micrograms of folic acid may reduce the risk of heart defects.
