Autism Spectrum Disorder

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a problem that affects a child’s nervous system and growth and development. It usually shows up during a child’s first 3 years of life.

Some children with ASD seem to live in their own world. They are not interested in other children and lack social awareness. A child with ASD focuses on following a routine that may include usual behaviors. A child with the disorder also often has problems communicating with others and may not start speaking as soon as other children. He or she may not want to make eye contact with other people.

ASD can keep a child from developing social skills. This is in part because a child with ASD may not be able to understand facial expressions or emotions in other people. A child with ASD may:

  • Not want to be touched
  • Want to play alone
  • Not want to change routines

A child with ASD may also repeat movements, such as flapping their hands or rocking. They may also have unusual attachments to objects. However, some children with ASD may do certain mental tasks very well, such as counting, or they may excel in areas like art or music and have a great memory for certain details.

Causes of ASD in a child

Researchers don’t know what causes ASD. It may be linked to certain genes. A child with ASD may also have structural problems in the brain or chemical imbalances. However, it is clear that ASD is not caused by the way parents raise their children.

In less common cases, other factors that may contribute to ASD include:

  • Exposure to toxins in the environment before or after birth
  • Problems during delivery
  • Infections before birth

Which children are at risk for ASD?

The disorder occurs much more often in boys than girls, with four to five times as many boys affected.

Certain gene disorders that run in families can increase a child's risk for ASD, including:

  • Fragile-X syndrome
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU)
  • Neurofibromatosis
  • Tuberous sclerosis
  • Chromosome problems

Your child may need genetic testing to determine if they have any of these problems. Testing is done by a medical geneticist, a healthcare provider with specialized training in genetic conditions. They can help you understand the chances of having another child with the same genetic condition. For instance, PKU carries a 1 in 4 chance of recurrence in future pregnancies, while tuberous sclerosis has a 1 in 2 chance.

Even when no specific gene problem is identified, having one child with ASD slightly increases the chances of having another child with ASD. Researchers believe this is because multiple genes from both parents may interact to cause ASD.

Symptoms

Each child may experience slightly different symptoms of ASD. However, common symptoms include:

Social Symptoms

  • Problems making eye contact with others
  • Difficulty making friends or interacting with other children

Communication Symptoms

  • Poor communication skills
  • Delayed speech development or no speech at all
  • Failure to use speech in social interactions
  • Repetitive language (echolalia) or repeating TV/movie phrases

Behavior Symptoms

  • Repetitive movements such as rocking or flapping hands
  • Heightened or diminished sensitivity to lights, sounds, touch, or taste
  • Rigid rituals
  • Strong need for routines

The symptoms of ASD may resemble other health conditions, so it's essential to consult with a healthcare provider for a diagnosis.

Diagnosis

No single medical test can diagnose ASD. Healthcare providers use certain guidelines to diagnose ASD in children before age 2. Early diagnosis allows treatment to begin as soon as possible, which can improve the child's development.

The guidelines recommend that all children be screened for ASD and other developmental disorders before age 2 during routine well-child checkups. Children with developmental or behavioral concerns will need additional testing for ASD.

Warning Signs

During well-child visits before age 2, healthcare providers look for signs such as:

  • No babbling, pointing, or gesturing by age 12 months
  • No single words spoken by age 16 months
  • No two-word phrases by age 24 months, only repeating sounds
  • Loss of language or social skills at any age
  • No eye contact by 3 to 4 months

If any of these issues are present, the healthcare provider will conduct further screenings to determine if the child has ASD or another developmental disorder.

Treatment

Every child with ASD requires a personalized treatment plan, which should begin as early as possible and involve the child's parents. Treatment may include:

  • Behavioral therapy: Programs that teach social, movement, and cognitive skills, and help change problematic behaviors.
  • Special education: Programs focusing on social skills, language, self-care, and vocational skills.
  • Medication: Some children may benefit from medications that help manage certain symptoms.

In addition, your family may need mental health support, such as parent counseling, social skills training, and one-on-one therapy. A healthcare provider can help you find the right treatment options for your child.

Prevention

There is no known way to prevent ASD. However, early diagnosis and intervention can help reduce symptoms and support a child’s development, ultimately improving their quality of life.

Living with Autism

Your child's primary care provider plays a key role in supporting both you and your child. They will help you understand treatment options and how to care for your child. Parents play a crucial role in the treatment and well-being of a child with ASD. Here are some things you can do:

  • Keep all appointments with your child’s healthcare provider.
  • Work with your child’s healthcare provider and schools to develop a treatment plan.
  • Reach out for support from local community services, which can provide assistance and connections with other parents of children with ASD.

Key Points

  • Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) affects a child's nervous system and development.
  • A child with ASD may have difficulty communicating and developing social skills.
  • Genetics may play a role in ASD.
  • All children should be screened for ASD by age 2.
  • Diagnosis may involve imaging and genetic testing.
  • Children with ASD need individualized treatment plans that may include behavioral programs, special education, and medication.
