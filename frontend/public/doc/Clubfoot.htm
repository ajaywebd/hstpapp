Clubfoot

Clubfoot is a deformity of the foot where one or both feet are turned inward. This condition affects the bones, muscles, tendons, and blood vessels. It is present at birth and tends to occur more frequently in boys than girls.

Causes

The exact cause of clubfoot is unknown (idiopathic), but it is believed to be due to a combination of genetic and environmental factors.

Risk Factors

  • Family history: If either parent or a sibling has had clubfoot, the baby is more likely to have it.
  • Congenital conditions: Clubfoot may be associated with other skeletal abnormalities like spina bifida.
  • Environment: Smoking during pregnancy, infections, or recreational drug use can increase the risk.
  • Amniotic fluid levels: Too little amniotic fluid during pregnancy may raise the likelihood of clubfoot.

Symptoms of Clubfoot

  • The top of the foot is twisted downward and inward, increasing the arch and turning the heel inward.
  • The foot may appear severely twisted, sometimes looking as if it is upside down.
  • The calf muscles in the affected leg are underdeveloped.
  • The affected foot may be up to 1/2 inch shorter than the other foot.

Despite the appearance, clubfoot itself does not cause pain or discomfort.

Diagnosis

Clubfoot is typically diagnosed immediately after birth based on the foot's shape and positioning. Occasionally, X-rays may be used to assess severity, but they are usually unnecessary. In some cases, clubfoot can be detected during prenatal ultrasound exams.

Treatment

The goal of treatment is to straighten the foot so it can grow and develop normally. Treatment options include:

  • Nonsurgical treatments: The Ponseti method, which involves gentle stretching and casting, is the most common treatment in the U.S. It typically takes 2 to 3 months. Other approaches include taping, physical therapy, and splinting.
  • Braces: Braces are used to prevent relapse. Initially, they are worn for 23 hours daily for up to 3 months, then only at night for 2 to 4 years.
  • Surgery: If nonsurgical treatments are ineffective, surgery may be necessary. Depending on the severity, surgical wires, pins, or a cast may be used to maintain the corrected position.

Most infants with clubfoot do not require surgery. For those who do, multiple procedures may be needed to address recurring deformities as the child grows.

Complications

Clubfoot typically doesn't cause significant problems until your child begins to stand and walk. With treatment, most children walk fairly normally but may face challenges such as:

  • Movement: Slightly limited mobility.
  • Shoe size: The affected foot may be up to 1 1/2 sizes smaller than the unaffected foot.
  • Calf size: Smaller calf muscles on the affected side.

However, untreated clubfoot can lead to more serious complications, including:

  • Arthritis: Increased likelihood of arthritis.
  • Poor self-image: The unusual appearance of the foot may impact self-esteem during the teen years.
  • Inability to walk normally: Severe twisting of the ankle may prevent walking on the sole of the foot, causing compensation by walking on the ball, outside, or top of the foot.
  • Walking adjustments: These may limit natural calf muscle growth, cause large sores or calluses, and result in an awkward gait.

Prevention

The exact cause of clubfoot is unknown, making complete prevention difficult. However, pregnant individuals can reduce the risk of birth defects by:

  • Not smoking or spending time in smoky environments.
  • Not drinking alcohol.
  • Avoiding drugs not approved by a doctor.

Key Points

  • Clubfoot is a deformity where one or both feet turn inward.
  • Children with a family history of clubfoot are at higher risk.
  • It causes the heel to point downward, and the forefoot turns inward, giving the foot a short, broad appearance.
  • Most cases are diagnosed at birth.
  • Treatment includes stretching and casting, with surgery as an option if other methods are ineffective.
