Botulism

Botulism (or botulism poisoning) is a rare but very serious illness that spreads through food, contact with contaminated soil, or an open wound. Without early treatment, botulism can lead to paralysis, breathing difficulties, and death.

Types of Botulism

  • Infant botulism
  • Foodborne botulism
  • Wound botulism

Botulism poisoning is caused by a toxin produced by the bacteria Clostridium botulinum. These bacteria thrive in oxygen-free environments, such as improperly canned foods. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) reports approximately 145 cases of botulism annually in the United States, with a 3 to 5 percent fatality rate.

Symptoms

Symptoms of botulism can appear between 6 hours and 10 days after infection. For infant and foodborne botulism, symptoms typically appear 12 to 36 hours after consuming contaminated food.

Early Signs of Infant Botulism

  • Constipation
  • Difficulty feeding
  • Tiredness
  • Irritability
  • Drooling
  • Drooping eyelids
  • Weak cry
  • Loss of head control and floppy movements due to muscle weakness
  • Paralysis

Signs of Foodborne or Wound Botulism

  • Difficulty swallowing or speaking
  • Facial weakness on both sides of the face
  • Blurred vision
  • Drooping eyelids
  • Trouble breathing
  • Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramps (only in foodborne botulism)
  • Paralysis

Causes

The CDC reports that 65 percent of botulism cases occur in infants or children younger than 1 year of age. Infant botulism is typically the result of exposure to contaminated soil or eating foods containing botulism spores. Honey and corn syrup are two examples of foods that can harbor botulism spores. These spores grow inside the intestinal tract of infants, releasing the botulism toxin. Older children and adults have natural defenses that prevent the bacteria from growing.

According to the CDC, around 15 percent of botulism cases are foodborne. These cases often result from home-canned or improperly processed commercially canned foods. The World Health Organization (WHO) identifies the following as potential sources of botulism toxin:

  • Preserved vegetables with low acid content, such as beets, spinach, mushrooms, and green beans
  • Canned tuna fish
  • Fermented, smoked, and salted fish
  • Meat products, such as ham and sausage

Wound botulism accounts for 20 percent of all botulism cases and occurs when botulism spores enter an open wound. This type has increased recently due to drug use, as spores are commonly present in heroin and cocaine.

Botulism is not passed from person to person. It requires the consumption of spores or toxin through food, or the toxin must enter a wound, to cause symptoms.

Diagnosis

If botulism is suspected, immediate medical attention is crucial. Early diagnosis and treatment are essential for survival. To diagnose botulism, a doctor will conduct a physical exam, noting any signs or symptoms. They will inquire about foods consumed within the past few days and check for any wounds.

In infants, the doctor will check for physical symptoms and inquire about foods eaten, such as honey or corn syrup. Blood or stool samples may be taken to analyze for toxins, although results may take days. Diagnosis often relies on clinical observation.

Additional Diagnostic Tests

  • Electromyography (EMG) to evaluate muscle response
  • Imaging scans to detect internal damage to the head or brain
  • Spinal fluid tests to determine if infection or injury to the brain or spinal cord is causing symptoms

Treatment

  • For foodborne and wound botulism, doctors administer an antitoxin as soon as possible after diagnosis.
  • In infants, botulism immune globulin blocks the neurotoxins circulating in the blood.
  • Severe cases may require a ventilator to support breathing, and recovery may take weeks or months.
  • Long-term therapy and rehabilitation may be necessary for severe cases.
  • There is a vaccine for botulism, but its effectiveness is not fully tested and has side effects.

Prevention

  • Follow proper canning techniques at home, ensuring adequate heat and acidity levels.
  • Be cautious with fermented fish or other aquatic game foods.
  • Discard open or bulging cans of commercially prepared food.
  • Refrigerate oils infused with garlic or herbs.
  • Keep cooked potatoes in aluminum foil hot or refrigerate them immediately.
  • Boil foods for 10 minutes to destroy botulism toxin.
  • Avoid feeding infants honey or corn syrup, as these may contain Clostridium botulinum spores.
