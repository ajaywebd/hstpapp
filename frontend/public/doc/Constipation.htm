Constipation in Children

Constipation in children is a common issue where they experience infrequent bowel movements or hard, dry stools. While early toilet training and dietary changes often contribute to constipation, most cases are temporary. Encouraging dietary adjustments, such as consuming fiber-rich fruits and vegetables and staying hydrated, can alleviate symptoms. In some cases, laxatives may be used with a doctor's approval.

Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of constipation in children may include:

  • Less than three bowel movements a week
  • Hard, dry, and difficult-to-pass stools
  • Large-diameter stools that may obstruct the toilet
  • Pain during bowel movements
  • Abdominal pain
  • Traces of liquid or clay-like stool in underwear
  • Blood on the surface of hard stool

If your child fears painful bowel movements, they may avoid using the toilet, displaying behaviors such as crossing legs, clenching buttocks, or twisting their body.

When to See a Doctor

While constipation in children is usually not serious, chronic constipation can lead to complications or indicate an underlying condition. Consult a doctor if constipation lasts more than two weeks or is accompanied by:

  • Fever
  • Vomiting
  • Blood in the stool
  • Abdominal swelling
  • Weight loss
  • Painful anal fissures
  • Intestinal protrusion from the anus (rectal prolapse)

Causes

Constipation occurs when stool moves too slowly through the digestive tract, causing it to become hard and dry. Contributing factors include:

  • Withholding: Children may ignore the urge to poop due to fear of the toilet, reluctance to stop playing, or previous painful bowel movements.
  • Toilet Training Issues: Starting toilet training too early or turning it into a battle of wills may lead to stool withholding becoming an involuntary habit.
  • Dietary Changes: Insufficient fiber or fluids, or transitioning from an all-liquid diet to solid foods, can lead to constipation.
  • Routine Changes: Travel, stress, hot weather, or starting school may disrupt bowel habits.
  • Medications: Certain antidepressants and other drugs can contribute to constipation.
  • Cow's Milk Allergy: An allergy to cow's milk or excessive dairy consumption may cause constipation.
  • Family History: A genetic or environmental predisposition may increase the risk of constipation in children.
  • Medical Conditions: Rarely, constipation may signal anatomic malformations or metabolic or digestive system issues.

Risk Factors

Constipation in children is more likely in kids who:

  • Are sedentary
  • Don't eat enough fiber
  • Don't drink enough fluids
  • Take certain medications, including some antidepressants
  • Have a medical condition affecting the anus or rectum
  • Have a family history of constipation

Diagnosis

Your child's doctor will typically perform the following to diagnose constipation:

  • Medical History: The doctor will ask about your child's past illnesses, diet, and activity patterns.
  • Physical Exam: This may include inserting a gloved finger into the anus to check for abnormalities or impacted stool. Stool may be tested for blood.

For severe cases, additional tests might include abdominal X-rays, anorectal manometry, barium enema X-rays, rectal biopsy, transit studies, or blood tests.

Treatment

Depending on the circumstances, treatments may include:

  • Fiber Supplements: Over-the-counter products like Metamucil or Citrucel may help, but your child needs to drink at least 32 ounces of water daily.
  • Glycerin Suppositories: These can soften stool for children unable to swallow pills.
  • Laxatives or Enemas: Products like polyethylene glycol (MiraLax) and mineral oil may be prescribed to clear blockages.
  • Hospital Enema: For severe cases, a stronger enema may be administered in the hospital.

Always consult your doctor before using laxatives or enemas and follow proper dosing instructions.

Complications

Chronic constipation may lead to:

  • Painful anal fissures
  • Rectal prolapse
  • Stool withholding
  • Encopresis (impacted stool leaking from the colon and rectum)

Prevention

To prevent constipation in children:

  • Offer high-fiber foods like fruits, vegetables, beans, and whole grains.
  • Encourage drinking plenty of water.
  • Promote regular physical activity.
  • Create a routine for using the toilet, especially after meals.
  • Remind your child to respond to the urge to poop promptly.
  • Review medications with your doctor if they contribute to constipation.
