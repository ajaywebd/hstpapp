Symptoms

  • More frequent urination, especially at night
  • Feeling tired and having less energy
  • Trouble sleeping
  • Nausea
  • Headaches
  • Weight loss and/or loss of appetite
  • Swelling in feet, ankles, or around the eyes due to fluid buildup

Causes

  • In infants and children: Birth defects, congenital abnormalities, and hereditary diseases like polycystic kidney disease. Frequent untreated urinary tract infections (UTIs) may also lead to CKD.
  • In teenagers: Conditions like glomerulonephritis, nephrotic syndrome, or systemic diseases like lupus are common causes.
  • In adults: Diabetes and high blood pressure are primary causes. While rare in children, obesity and type 2 diabetes beginning in childhood may contribute to CKD later in life.

Diagnosis

  • Urinalysis: Checks for protein in the urine, indicating kidney damage.
  • Blood tests: Assess kidney function, chemical levels, and red blood cell count.
  • Imaging: Ultrasounds and X-rays provide a visual assessment of kidney damage.
  • Kidney biopsy: Examines tissue samples to determine the cause and extent of kidney damage.

Treatment

Treatment starts with dietary changes and medications, including vitamins, calcium, and blood pressure pills. Injectable treatments like erythropoietin help manage anemia and boost energy, while human growth hormone injections promote normal growth. For advanced stages, options include dialysis or kidney transplant.

Treatment

Treatment begins with dietary changes and medicines. Children may need several medicines, including vitamins, calcium, bicarbonate, and blood pressure pills, which can make medication management challenging. Tools like medicine clocks or setting alarms can help children remember to take their medicines on time.

Injectable medicines, such as erythropoetin, are available for treating anemia and growth failure in children with chronic kidney disease. These injections can improve energy levels and support normal growth. Human growth hormone injections are also effective in promoting better growth for many children with CKD.

Dialysis

For children with end-stage kidney disease, dialysis is often necessary until a donor kidney becomes available. The two main types of dialysis are:

  • Hemodialysis: Blood is filtered outside the body using a machine. This procedure typically takes several hours and is done three or more times a week, either at a dialysis center or at home in some cases.
  • Peritoneal dialysis: Uses the body's peritoneal membrane to filter blood. Two forms are available:
    • Continuous cycling peritoneal dialysis (CCPD): Performed at night using a cycler machine, often requiring parental assistance, suitable for younger children.
    • Continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis (CAPD): Performed during the day without a machine, typically managed by older children or teens.

Peritoneal dialysis generally allows for fewer dietary restrictions, offering more flexibility and promoting better growth in children compared to hemodialysis.

Helping Your Child

Kids with chronic kidney disease often need dietary changes to ensure they get enough calories and nutrients. Extra carbohydrates and fats can increase calorie intake. However, intake of water, salt, potassium, and dairy may need to be limited due to their effects on kidney function.

  • Phosphorus restriction: High phosphorus levels can cause calcium deposits in the body and leach calcium from bones, increasing the risk of fractures. Reducing dairy and other protein-rich foods can ease kidney workload but should be balanced to support growth.
  • Fluid intake monitoring: As kidney function declines, fluid intake may need to be limited. Smaller drink portions and alternatives like slushy beverages or ice cubes can help manage fluid intake.

Sodium

Children with kidney disease, especially those with high blood pressure, may need to restrict sodium intake. Sodium is found in table salt and many processed foods. Be cautious with salt substitutes, as they often contain potassium, which can be problematic for kids with kidney failure. Even "natural salts" like Himalayan salts are high in sodium chloride.

Always read food labels and consult your doctor or dietitian to determine the sodium content in various foods. A nephrologist can recommend a diet that meets your child's calorie and nutrient needs while minimizing kidney damage and avoiding complications.

Exercise

Exercise helps kids sweat, eliminating excess fluids and flushing out toxins through the skin. Limit screen time from TVs and video games, and encourage physical activity. Activities like walking and strength training can strengthen bones and stimulate muscles and nerves, easing issues like "restless leg syndrome" associated with kidney disease.

Emotions

Supporting emotional health is crucial for kids with chronic kidney disease. Encourage them to express their feelings and connect with well-adjusted young adults who managed kidney disease during childhood. Nephrologists or organizations like the National Kidney Foundation can help you find support groups.

Participation in school and extracurricular activities can boost self-esteem. During hemodialysis treatments, positive activities like homework, reading, or art projects can help kids pass the time constructively.

Looking Ahead

As children grow older, they can take more responsibility for their care. School-age kids should know their medications and how to take them. Teens transitioning to adulthood can help schedule appointments and should be encouraged to discuss their condition with their health care team independently.

Helping children talk about their condition with teachers, coaches, and friends is a significant step. Teens often struggle with not wanting to stand out, so fostering independence while identifying limitations is important.

Long-term medication use, such as prednisone, can have side effects, including weight gain, mood changes, sleep disturbances, cataracts, and weakened bones. These side effects can also affect growth and pubertal development. For teens, visible side effects like acne can impact body image. It is important to address these concerns alongside kidney disease management.
