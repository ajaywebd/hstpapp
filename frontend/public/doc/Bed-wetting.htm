Bed-wetting

Bed-wetting, also known as nighttime incontinence or nocturnal enuresis, is involuntary urination during sleep after the age at which nighttime bladder control is typically developed. It is common among children and often just a normal part of development.

While bed-wetting before age 7 is usually not a concern, ongoing bed-wetting can be addressed with patience, understanding, and methods such as lifestyle changes, bladder training, moisture alarms, and medication.

Symptoms

Most children achieve toilet training by age 5, but some may continue to experience bed-wetting between the ages of 5 and 7. After age 7, a small number of children may still wet the bed.

Causes

Bed-wetting can occur due to various factors, including:

  • A small bladder: The bladder may not be fully developed to hold nighttime urine.
  • Inability to recognize a full bladder: Immature bladder nerves may fail to wake the child.
  • Hormone imbalance: A lack of anti-diuretic hormone (ADH) may increase nighttime urine production.
  • Urinary tract infection: Symptoms include bed-wetting, frequent urination, and pain during urination.
  • Sleep apnea: Obstructive sleep apnea, often due to enlarged tonsils or adenoids, can cause bed-wetting.
  • Diabetes: Bed-wetting can be an early sign of diabetes, accompanied by excessive thirst and fatigue.
  • Chronic constipation: Dysfunctional muscles due to long-term constipation can contribute to bed-wetting.
  • Structural problems: Rarely, defects in the urinary or nervous system may be the cause.

Risk Factors

Bed-wetting is twice as common in boys as in girls. Risk factors include:

  • Stress and anxiety: Life changes such as becoming a sibling or starting school can trigger bed-wetting.
  • Family history: Children with parents who experienced bed-wetting are more likely to wet the bed.
  • ADHD: Bed-wetting is more common in children with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder.

Complications

Although bed-wetting typically doesn’t cause physical health issues, it can lead to:

  • Guilt and embarrassment: These feelings can lower self-esteem.
  • Loss of social opportunities: Bed-wetting may prevent participation in activities like sleepovers or camps.
  • Rashes: Prolonged contact with wet underwear can cause skin irritation or rashes.

Diagnosis

To identify the underlying cause of bed-wetting, your doctor may recommend the following:

  • Physical exam
  • Discussion of symptoms, fluid intake, family history, bowel and bladder habits, and associated problems
  • Urine tests to check for infections or diabetes
  • Imaging tests of the kidneys or bladder to examine urinary tract structure
  • Other urinary tract assessments, as needed

Treatment

Treatment depends on the child’s symptoms and underlying causes. Most children outgrow bed-wetting naturally, but options may include:

Moisture Alarms

  • Battery-operated devices that alert the child when wetness is detected
  • Helps train the child to wake and use the toilet
  • Requires consistent use for 1-3 months
  • Low risk of relapse or side effects

Medication

  • Desmopressin (DDAVP): Reduces nighttime urine production but must be used cautiously.
  • Oxybutynin (Ditropan XL): Relaxes bladder muscles, increasing capacity for children with small bladders.
  • Medications may not cure the problem but can provide temporary relief.

Lifestyle and Home Remedies

  • Limit fluids in the evening.
  • Avoid beverages with caffeine.
  • Encourage double voiding before bed.
  • Promote regular toilet use throughout the day.
  • Prevent rashes with morning hygiene and protective ointments.

Alternative Medicine

Alternative treatments such as hypnosis, acupuncture, or herbal therapy have limited evidence of effectiveness. Consult your doctor before trying these methods.

Coping and Support

  • Be patient and supportive. Bed-wetting is involuntary and not the child’s fault.
  • Plan for easy cleanup with waterproof mattress covers and extra bedding.
  • Encourage your child to help with cleanup to foster a sense of control.
  • Celebrate effort, not results. Use reward systems to motivate your child.
