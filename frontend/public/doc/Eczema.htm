Eczema

Eczema (eg-zuh-MUH) refers to a group of conditions causing red, itchy, and inflamed skin. Common types include atopic dermatitis, contact dermatitis, dyshidrotic eczema, and seborrheic dermatitis (or "cradle cap" in infants). It often appears in children between 6 months and 5 years old. Eczema is not contagious and cannot be "caught" from someone else.

While there is no cure, managing eczema involves avoiding triggers, daily skin care, and using medications. Treatment options include over-the-counter remedies, prescription topical medications, phototherapy, and immunosuppressants.

Causes

Eczema triggers can vary but commonly include:

  • Dry skin
  • Irritants
  • Heat and sweating
  • Infections
  • Allergens like pet dander, pollen, or dust

Winter dryness and drooling can worsen symptoms, particularly on the face, chin, and neck in babies. Identifying your child's triggers is essential for managing eczema effectively.

Symptoms

Eczema symptoms and their locations may change with age. In infants, it typically appears on the face, elbows, and knees. In older children and adults, eczema is more common on the hands, feet, arms, and behind the knees.

Common symptoms include:

  • Dry, scaly skin
  • Small bumps that ooze when scratched
  • Redness and swelling
  • Thickened skin (with chronic eczema)

Excessive scratching can tear the skin and lead to infections. Some children may have occasional flare-ups, while others experience symptoms into adulthood.

Diagnosis

Eczema is common, affecting at least 10 percent of infants and children, according to the National Institute of Arthritis and Musculoskeletal and Skin Diseases. Diagnosis typically involves a physical exam and medical history. Other factors may include:

  • Family history of allergic conditions
  • Personal history of allergies or asthma
  • Blood tests

Overview of Treatment

Specific treatment for eczema will be determined by the child's physician based on:

  • The child's age, overall health, and medical history
  • Extent of the disorder
  • The child's tolerance for specific medications, procedures, or therapies
  • Expectations for the course of the reaction
  • Child or parent's opinion or preference

Note: There is no cure for eczema. The primary goals of treatment are to reduce itching and inflammation, moisturize the skin, and prevent infection.

Developing Treatments

A new class of medications, called topical immunomodulators (TIMs), offers a steroid-free option for treating atopic dermatitis. These medications relieve itching, redness, and pain caused by eczema and can be used anywhere on the body.

Tacrolimus is the first TIM approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Another promising TIM is pimecrolimus. However, the FDA advises against using TIMs on children under two years old. Always consult with the child's physician for detailed information.

How to Manage Eczema

The following are suggestions for managing eczema effectively:

  • Avoid contact with irritants as determined by the child's physician
  • Bathe the child regularly, use moisturizers, and practice good skin care techniques
  • Keep the child's room cool at night to prevent sweating, which can irritate the skin
  • Avoid harsh soaps; ask the child's physician to recommend a brand
  • Keep the child's fingernails short to minimize scratching that may lead to infection
  • Apply lubricating lotions at least once daily; consult the child's physician for recommended brands

Treatment for Severe Eczema

In severe cases, the child's physician may prescribe medications. Common treatments include:

  • Antihistamines: These medications reduce itching. Examples include diphenhydramine (Benadryl®) and hydroxyzine (Atarax®). They may cause drowsiness, but newer non-drowsy options are available. Consult the physician for recommendations.
  • Steroid creams: These topical medications help reduce skin inflammation, itching, and swelling. They come in various strengths and should be used according to the physician's advice, as overuse may damage the skin.
  • Oral antibiotics: These may be prescribed if an infection occurs due to excessive scratching or broken skin.
