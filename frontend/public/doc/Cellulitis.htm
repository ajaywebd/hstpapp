Cellulitis

Cellulitis is a common bacterial skin infection that typically appears as a red, swollen area that feels hot and tender to the touch. The redness and swelling can spread quickly, and the condition is usually painful. While cellulitis commonly affects the skin on the lower legs, it can occur anywhere on the body or face. It may involve the surface of the skin, underlying tissues, lymph nodes, and even the bloodstream.

If untreated, cellulitis can spread and become life-threatening. Immediate medical attention is necessary if you experience symptoms of cellulitis.

Symptoms

  • Pain and tenderness in the affected area
  • Redness or inflammation of the skin
  • A skin sore or rash that appears and grows quickly
  • A tight, glossy, swollen appearance of the skin
  • A feeling of warmth in the affected area
  • Central area with abscess and pus formation
  • Fever

More serious symptoms of cellulitis infection include:

  • Shaking
  • Chills
  • Feeling unwell
  • Fatigue
  • Dizziness
  • Lightheadedness
  • Muscle aches
  • Warm skin
  • Sweating

Symptoms indicating that cellulitis is spreading:

  • Drowsiness
  • Lethargy
  • Blistering
  • Red streaks

Contact your doctor immediately if any of these symptoms occur.

Causes and Risk Factors

Cellulitis occurs when certain bacteria, commonly Staphylococcus or Streptococcus, enter through a cut or crack in the skin. It often develops at sites of skin injuries such as cuts, insect bites, or surgical incisions.

Risk factors for cellulitis include:

  • A weakened immune system
  • Skin conditions causing breaks in the skin, such as eczema or athlete’s foot
  • Intravenous (IV) drug use
  • Diabetes
  • A history of cellulitis

Diagnosis

A doctor can usually diagnose cellulitis by physical examination. This may reveal:

  • Swelling of the skin
  • Redness and warmth in the affected area
  • Swollen glands

Depending on the severity, the doctor may monitor the area for a few days to observe any spread of redness or swelling. In some cases, blood tests or wound cultures may be performed to confirm the presence of bacteria.

Treatment

Your doctor will typically prescribe a 10- to 21-day regimen of oral antibiotics to treat cellulitis. The duration of the treatment will depend on the severity of the condition. Even if symptoms improve within a few days, it's crucial to complete the entire prescribed course to ensure proper treatment. Monitor your condition during this time to ensure symptoms improve. In most cases, symptoms should improve or disappear within a few days.

In some cases, pain relievers may be prescribed. Rest is essential until symptoms improve. While resting, elevate the affected limb higher than your heart to reduce swelling.

Contact your doctor immediately if you don’t respond to treatment within three days of starting antibiotics, if symptoms worsen, or if you develop a fever. Cellulitis should resolve within seven to 10 days of starting antibiotics. Severe infections may require longer treatment, especially in individuals with chronic diseases or weakened immune systems.

Hospitalization may be necessary for individuals with certain medical conditions or risk factors. Your doctor may recommend hospitalization if you:

  • Have a high temperature
  • Have high blood pressure
  • Have an infection that doesn’t improve with antibiotics
  • Have a compromised immune system due to other diseases
  • Require IV antibiotics when oral antibiotics don’t work

Possible Complications

In some cases, cellulitis can spread throughout the body, affecting the lymph nodes and bloodstream. Rarely, it can spread to deeper tissue layers. Potential complications include:

  • A blood infection
  • A bone infection
  • Inflammation of lymph vessels
  • Tissue death, or gangrene

Preventing Cellulitis

To prevent cellulitis, clean any break in the skin immediately and apply antibiotic ointment regularly. Cover the wound with a bandage and change it daily until a scab forms. Monitor wounds for signs of infection, such as redness, drainage, or pain.

Individuals with poor circulation or pre-existing conditions that increase the risk of cellulitis should take extra precautions, including:

  • Keeping skin moisturized to prevent cracking
  • Promptly treating superficial skin infections, such as athlete's foot
  • Wearing protective equipment during activities
  • Inspecting feet daily for signs of injury or infection
