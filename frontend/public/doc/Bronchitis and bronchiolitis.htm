Bronchitis and Bronchiolitis

Bronchitis is an inflammation of the lining of your bronchial tubes, which carry air to and from your lungs. People with bronchitis often cough up thickened mucus, which can be discolored. Bronchitis may be either acute or chronic.

Bronchiolitis is a common lung infection in young children and infants. It causes inflammation and congestion in the small airways (bronchioles) of the lungs. Bronchiolitis is almost always caused by a virus, typically peaking during the winter months.

Bronchiolitis starts with symptoms similar to a common cold but progresses to coughing, wheezing, and sometimes difficulty breathing. Symptoms can last for several days to weeks. Most children recover with home care, but some may require hospitalization.

Symptoms of Bronchitis and Bronchiolitis

Bronchitis and bronchiolitis share common symptoms such as coughing, wheezing, and slight fever. However, there are unique symptoms for each condition.

Bronchitis Symptoms

  • Cough
  • Production of mucus
  • Shortness of breath
  • Slight fever
  • Chills
  • Fatigue
  • Chest discomfort or tightness

Contact your doctor if your child's cough lasts more than three weeks or prevents them from sleeping. Call the doctor if the cough is accompanied by:

  • Wheezing
  • Bright yellow or green mucus (indicating infection)
  • Blood-tinged sputum

Bronchiolitis Symptoms

  • Dry, raspy cough
  • Wheezing
  • Difficulty feeding, especially in infants
  • Slight fever
  • Runny or stuffy nose

Call your pediatrician if your child has the following symptoms:

  • Difficulty breathing
  • Breathing rate of 50-60 breaths per minute
  • Temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher
  • Uncharacteristic tiredness or irritability
  • No diaper change in 12 hours or more
  • Eating less than half their typical amount during the last several meals
  • Bluish color to the face

Causes

Both conditions can be caused by viruses.

Acute bronchitis is often caused by viruses, especially the flu and the common cold. The most common cause of chronic bronchitis is smoking cigarettes or being around lung irritants.

Bronchiolitis is typically caused by a viral infection. The respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is responsible for more than half of all cases of bronchiolitis. The common cold or flu can also cause the condition. Bronchitis can also have bacterial origins.

Risk Factors

Being around cigarette smoke and other lung irritants is a risk factor for both conditions.

Risk factors specific to bronchitis include:

  • Gastric reflux, which can irritate your throat
  • Regular exposure to irritants
  • Low immune resistance, especially after a virus or primary infection

High-risk factors for developing bronchiolitis include:

  • Babies born prematurely
  • Babies with heart, lung, or immune conditions
  • Children who spend time in day care

Diagnosis

When your doctor suspects bronchitis, they will listen to the lungs with a stethoscope and ask about other symptoms. If pneumonia is suspected, they may order a chest X-ray, which can also rule out other conditions like lung cancer.

Additional tests may include:

  • Sputum tests to send to the lab for a culture
  • A pulmonary function test to measure lung performance and breathing ability

If your doctor suspects that your child has bronchiolitis, they will listen to the lungs with a stethoscope, review the medical history, and note any symptoms. They may also:

  • Collect a mucus sample to check for viruses
  • Run a blood test to check white blood cell count, which indicates infection

Chest X-rays are rarely used to diagnose bronchiolitis unless complications are suspected.

Treatment

Treatments for bronchitis and bronchiolitis look very different.

Treatment for Bronchitis

  • Antibiotics to treat bacterial infections
  • Cough medicine, only for use at night to aid sleeping
  • Medications such as steroids to reduce inflammation for conditions like asthma
  • Medications by inhalation to help open the airways
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation, which includes a breathing exercise program for chronic bronchitis

Treatment for Bronchiolitis

  • Helping your baby sleep with their head slightly raised by putting a pillow under the mattress
  • Encouraging your child to drink as much fluid as possible
  • Providing supplemental oxygen or IV fluids if hospitalized

Outlook

Bronchitis typically isn’t a cause for concern but may lead to complications like pneumonia. Recurrent cases could indicate chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), requiring prompt treatment.

Many cases of bronchiolitis are minor and can be treated at home. However, untreated severe bronchiolitis can cause recurrent wheezing and reduced quality of life into teenage years. The condition may also be fatal if not treated.

Most children recover at home within three to five days. If hospitalized, recovery may take up to a week.

Prevention

To prevent both conditions:

  • Avoid cigarette smoke and other environmental irritants
  • Practice good hygiene by washing hands regularly
  • Wipe down children’s toys regularly

For bronchitis prevention, getting vaccinated with the annual flu vaccine can help reduce the risk, as post-flu infections cause many cases of bronchitis.
