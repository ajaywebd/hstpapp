Food Allergies

What is a food allergy?

A food allergy occurs when the immune system mistakenly treats a certain food as dangerous. Antibodies to the allergen trigger the release of chemicals like histamine, which can cause symptoms that affect various parts of the body.

What's a reaction like?

For example, in a peanut allergy, antibodies react to peanuts, causing symptoms such as:

  • Tightness in the throat
  • Hoarse voice
  • Wheezing
  • Cough
  • Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea
  • Hives or tingling in the lips or tongue

In severe cases, a food allergy can cause anaphylaxis, a life-threatening condition where the body’s systems react all at once, potentially causing blood pressure drops, airway constriction, and swelling of the tongue.

How do you know if you have one?

It can be difficult to determine, as food allergies may be hard to identify when multiple ingredients are involved. Doctors may conduct allergy tests or review family history, as food allergies can be hereditary. They may also run diagnostic tests such as a skin test using extracts of food allergens to see how the body reacts.

What will the doctor do?

If a doctor suspects a food allergy, you will be referred to an allergist, who will ask about reactions and family history. The allergist may conduct a skin or liquid extract test to confirm allergies.

How do food allergies get diagnosed?

If you think you may have a food allergy, it's important to tell a parent and get a doctor’s evaluation. The allergist will ask about your reactions, family history, and symptoms. Diagnosis often involves a skin test where the doctor will apply a small amount of different food extracts to check for reactions like redness or swelling. They may also perform a blood test to check for specific antibodies.

How are food allergies treated?

There is no cure for food allergies, but there are ways to manage them:

  • Avoidance: The most effective way to handle a food allergy is by avoiding the trigger food.
  • Emergency Plan: It’s important to have a plan in case of accidental exposure. This plan often includes carrying epinephrine (an adrenaline shot) for severe reactions like anaphylaxis.
  • Antihistamines: These can help manage mild symptoms, though they aren’t a substitute for epinephrine in severe cases.

Living with food allergies

Living with food allergies means being cautious, but it doesn’t have to slow you down. Many companies now make allergen-free substitutes for favourite foods, making it easier to manage.
