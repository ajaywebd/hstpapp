Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)

An atrial septal defect (ASD) is an opening in the atrial septum, the wall between the heart's upper chambers, the right and left atria. ASD can be congenital (present at birth) or result from the failure of normal postnatal closure of a fetal hole. This defect allows oxygen-rich blood to pass from the left atrium to the right atrium, mixing with oxygen-poor blood.

Atrial septal defects occur in a small percentage of children born with congenital heart disease. Girls are twice as likely as boys to have ASDs.

Causes

ASDs usually occur during fetal development when the heart partitions don’t fully form, leaving an opening in the atrial septum. Some congenital heart defects may have genetic links or result from environmental exposure, although most ASDs occur sporadically without a clear cause.

Types of Atrial Septal Defects

  • Ostium secundum ASD: The most common type, resulting from an incomplete closure in the central part of the septum.
  • Ostium primum ASD: Associated with atrioventricular canal defects and often involves a cleft in the mitral valve.
  • Sinus venosus ASD: Occurs near the superior vena cava and the junction with the right atrium, causing abnormal drainage of the pulmonary veins.
  • Coronary sinus ASD: Located in the coronary sinus wall, this rare defect involves the area where the coronary veins drain into the right atrium.

Why is an ASD a Concern?

An ASD can lead to lung problems if left untreated, as the right side of the heart must handle an increased blood volume, which then flows into the lungs. Over time, this can lead to high blood pressure in the lungs and irreversible lung damage.

Symptoms

While many children show no symptoms, larger ASDs may cause:

  • Easy fatigue during play
  • Fatigue
  • Sweating
  • Rapid breathing
  • Shortness of breath
  • Poor growth
  • Frequent respiratory infections

ASD symptoms can resemble other heart conditions, so a physician should be consulted for a proper diagnosis.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis may involve a medical history review and physical examination, along with various procedures:

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG): Measures heart electrical activity and detects arrhythmias. Variations include:
    • Resting ECG: Performed while lying down, with electrodes placed on the chest, arms, and legs to record heart activity.
    • Exercise ECG (stress test): Performed while the child exercises to assess heart function under stress.
    • Holter monitor: A portable ECG worn for 24+ hours to record heart activity continuously. Two types:
      • Continuous recording: Records throughout the testing period.
      • Event monitor: Records only during symptoms or abnormal rhythms.
  • Electrophysiologic study (EPS): An invasive test using a catheter to find the arrhythmia's origin. An esophageal electrophysiologic study may also be used, with a thin tube inserted into the esophagus for precise ECG recording.
  • Tilt table test: Recommended for children with frequent fainting episodes to observe heart rate and blood pressure response to positional changes. Medication may be given intravenously during the test to prevent fainting.

Treatment

Treatment for an atrial septal defect (ASD) depends on your child's symptoms, age, and general health, as well as the severity of the condition. The most common type of ASD may close on its own as the child grows.

Once ASD is diagnosed, the cardiologist will monitor the child to see if the defect closes on its own. An ASD will usually be repaired if it hasn’t closed by the time a child reaches school age, especially if the defect is large.

Treatment may include:

  • Medicine: Many children show no symptoms and don’t need medication, but some benefit from medicines that support heart function, such as diuretics to help the kidneys eliminate extra fluid.
  • Surgery: In some cases, the ASD is repaired surgically under general anesthesia, using stitches or a special patch to close the defect.
  • Device closure: Some children benefit from this procedure, where a septal occluder device is placed in the ASD through cardiac catheterization, stopping the abnormal blood flow.

Living with Atrial Septal Defect

All children with an ASD require care from a pediatric cardiologist. Most children who undergo ASD repair live healthy lives. Post-repair, the doctor may prescribe antibiotics to prevent bacterial endocarditis, an infection of the heart lining.

Early diagnosis and repair generally lead to excellent outcomes, with minimal follow-up care required. Children diagnosed with ASD later in life or who experience complications post-repair may face greater risks. Some children may develop pulmonary hypertension and should receive follow-up care at a congenital heart disease center.

Consult your child’s healthcare provider for personalized information about your child's prognosis.

Key Points

  • An atrial septal defect is an opening in the wall dividing the two upper heart chambers.
  • Symptoms of ASD include fatigue, fast breathing, shortness of breath, poor growth, arrhythmias, and frequent respiratory infections.
  • Atrial septal defects vary in size, from small to large.
  • Small ASDs may close on their own over time.
  • Large ASDs or those causing symptoms can be repaired.
  • Most children who have had ASD repair will lead healthy lives.

Danger Signs

Contact your child's healthcare provider if new symptoms arise or if existing symptoms worsen. Symptoms may include:

  • Increased tiredness
  • Trouble breathing
  • Fast breathing
  • Racing heartbeat or palpitations
