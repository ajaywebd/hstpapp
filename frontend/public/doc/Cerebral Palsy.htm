Cerebral Palsy

Cerebral palsy (CP) is a brain (neurological) disorder that causes problems with normal motor function. It is a lifelong condition that affects how the brain and muscles communicate. CP affects body movement, muscle control, coordination, reflexes, posture, and balance. These problems are caused by damage to or abnormal development of certain brain areas. Types of Cerebral Palsy

  • Spastic cerebral palsy - causes stiffness and movement difficulties
  • Dyskinetic (athetoid) cerebral palsy - leads to involuntary and uncontrolled movements
  • Ataxic cerebral palsy - causes problems with balance and depth perception

Causes

CP occurs when there is abnormal development or damage to brain areas controlling motor function. This can happen before or during birth (congenital CP) or, less commonly, after birth (acquired CP). The exact cause is often unknown but may include:

  • Lack of oxygen to the brain
  • Genetic conditions
  • Infections
  • Bleeding in the brain
  • Severe jaundice
  • Head injury

Children at Risk

  • Preterm birth
  • Inflammation of the placenta or amniotic fluid (chorioamnionitis)
  • Blood clotting disorders
  • Very low birth weight
  • Exposure to infections or harmful substances during pregnancy
  • Complications during labor and delivery

Symptoms

Symptoms of CP vary widely and may include muscle weakness, poor motor control, or involuntary movements. Children with CP may also have additional problems such as seizures, vision or hearing issues, learning disabilities, and bowel or bladder problems.

  • Spastic diplegia: Spastic movements in the arms or legs
  • Spastic quadriplegia: Spastic movements in all four limbs
  • Spastic hemiplegia: Spasticity affecting one side of the body
  • Athetoid: Involuntary and purposeless movements
  • Ataxic: Issues with balance and coordination

Diagnosis

Diagnosis typically occurs between 6 and 12 months of age when developmental milestones like walking and head control are expected. Diagnostic methods include:

  • Neurological examinations
  • X-rays
  • Feeding studies
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG)
  • Blood tests
  • Gait lab analysis
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
  • Computed tomography (CT) scans
  • Genetic studies
  • Metabolic tests

Treatment

Specific treatment for cerebral palsy will be determined by your child's doctor based on:

  • Your child's age, overall health, and medical history
  • The extent of the disease
  • The type of CP
  • Your child's tolerance for specific medications, procedures, or therapies
  • Expectations for the course of the disease
  • Your opinion or preference

Since CP is a lifelong condition that is not correctable, management focuses on preventing or minimizing deformities and maximizing the child's capability at home and in the community. An interdisciplinary team often treats children with CP, including the following specialists:

  • Pediatrician/family practitioner
  • Orthopedic surgeon
  • Neurologist
  • Neurosurgeon
  • Ophthalmologist
  • Dentist
  • Nurse
  • Orthotist
  • Rehabilitation team (physical, occupational, speech therapy, audiology)

Management Options

  • Nonsurgical Interventions: Rehabilitation, positioning aids, braces, medications for seizures or muscle spasticity
  • Surgical Interventions: Address orthopedic problems, spasticity, and severe deformities like scoliosis

Long-Term Outlook

Since CP is a lifelong condition, management focuses on preventing deformities and promoting independence. Positive reinforcement strengthens self-esteem and helps children maximize their capabilities. The full extent of the condition may not be understood until the child grows.

Associated Problems

  • Intellectual Disability: Some children may have intellectual disabilities or learning disorders, while others may have normal intelligence.
  • Seizures: Affect one in three children with CP, often controllable with medication.
  • Vision Difficulties: Strabismus and other issues may require early correction to prevent permanent damage.
  • Limb Shortening and Scoliosis: Differences in limb length and curvature of the spine may need surgical intervention or orthotic support.
  • Dental Problems: Poor oral hygiene and enamel defects increase the risk of cavities and gum disease.
  • Hearing Loss: Some children with CP have partial or complete hearing loss, especially if caused by severe jaundice or oxygen deficiency at birth.
  • Joint Problems: Spasticity may cause joint contractures, requiring therapy or braces for improved mobility.
  • Problems with Spatial Awareness: Many children with one-sided CP struggle to sense the position of their affected limbs, requiring therapy to improve functionality.
