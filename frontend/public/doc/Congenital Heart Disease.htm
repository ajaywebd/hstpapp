Congenital Heart Disease (CHD)

Congenital heart disease refers to a variety of structural heart abnormalities present at birth. It occurs when specific steps in heart development during pregnancy are disrupted. Affecting 1 in 120 babies in the U.S., CHD is the most common birth defect. Understanding these classifications helps parents and healthcare providers manage and treat these conditions effectively.

Problems That Cause Too Much Blood to Pass Through the Lungs

These defects allow oxygen-rich blood to re-circulate through the lungs, leading to increased lung pressure and congestion.

  • Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA): An open passageway between the pulmonary artery and aorta fails to close after birth, leading to mixed blood flow.
  • Atrial Septal Defect (ASD): An abnormal opening between the right and left atria causes irregular blood flow.
  • Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD): A hole between the right and left ventricles allows oxygenated blood to flow back to the lungs.
  • Atrioventricular Canal (AVC): A combination of ASD, VSD, and malformed heart valves.

Problems That Cause Too Little Blood to Pass Through the Lungs

These defects prevent enough oxygen-poor blood from reaching the lungs for oxygenation, resulting in cyanosis (blue skin).

  • Tricuspid Atresia: Absence of the tricuspid valve prevents blood flow from the right atrium to the right ventricle.
  • Pulmonary Atresia: Malformation of the pulmonary valve restricts blood flow to the lungs.
  • Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA): The pulmonary artery and aorta are reversed.
  • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF): A combination of four defects, including VSD and narrowed pulmonary valve.
  • Double Outlet Right Ventricle (DORV): Both the aorta and pulmonary artery arise from the right ventricle.
  • Truncus Arteriosus: The aorta and pulmonary artery fail to separate properly, leaving a shared connection.

Problems That Cause Too Little Blood to Travel to the Body

These defects involve underdeveloped heart chambers or blockages in blood vessels, limiting blood supply to the body.

  • Coarctation of the Aorta (CoA): Narrowing of the aorta restricts blood flow to the lower body and increases upper-body blood pressure.
  • Aortic Stenosis (AS): Narrowing of the aortic valve hampers blood flow from the left ventricle to the body.
  • Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome (HLHS): Underdevelopment of the left side of the heart prevents effective blood pumping.

Complex Congenital Heart Defects

Some cases involve combinations of several defects, requiring specialized diagnosis and treatment plans tailored to the child's unique needs.

Causes of Congenital Heart Disease

Many congenital heart diseases have genetic causes, while others may involve environmental factors. Some known associations include:

  • Chromosome abnormalities, such as Down syndrome, trisomy 18, trisomy 13, and Turner's syndrome
  • Microdeletion or microduplication syndromes, such as DiGeorge syndrome
  • Single gene defects
  • Environmental factors

If you have a congenital heart defect, it is advisable to speak with a genetic counselor or genetic specialist before becoming pregnant.

Symptoms of Congenital Heart Defects

Symptoms of congenital heart defects in infants and children may include:

  • Cyanosis (a bluish tint to the skin, fingernails, and lips)
  • Respiratory distress
  • Poor feeding
  • Poor weight gain
  • Recurrent lung infections
  • Reduced ability to exercise or play actively compared to other children

Testing and Diagnosis

Serious congenital heart diseases are often detected during pregnancy via routine ultrasound exams. Other conditions may be diagnosed shortly after birth or later in childhood when symptoms become apparent.

If a physician suspects CHD during pregnancy, you may be referred to a pediatric cardiologist for further testing. Diagnostic tests can include:

  • Fetal echocardiogram
  • Electrocardiogram (EKG)
  • Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
  • Cardiac catheterization

At specialized centers, such as The Children's Hospital of Philadelphia, testing may be coordinated through programs like the Cardiac Center's Fetal Heart Program.

Treatments for Congenital Heart Disease

Treatments for CHD vary based on the severity of the condition. Some heart problems may be managed with medications, while others require surgical intervention or cardiac catheterization.

Certain simpler conditions, like patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) or atrial septal defect (ASD), may resolve on their own as the child grows. However, more complex conditions often require multiple operations, catheter interventions, and ongoing care.

Outlook and Follow-Up Care

Children with CHD are monitored by pediatric cardiologists who diagnose and manage their heart conditions before and after surgery. Surgical repairs are typically performed by pediatric cardiovascular or cardiothoracic surgeons.

It is crucial for individuals born with CHD to transition to appropriate adult cardiac care as they age. Community adult cardiologists can manage simpler CHD cases, while specialized care is required for more complex conditions.
