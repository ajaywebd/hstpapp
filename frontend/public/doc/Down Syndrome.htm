Down Syndrome

Down syndrome is a genetic disorder. It is also called trisomy 21. It includes certain birth defects, learning problems, and facial features. A child with Down syndrome also may have heart defects and problems with vision and hearing. How severe or mild these problems are varies from child to child.

Down syndrome is one of the most common genetic birth defects. It affects about 1 in 800 babies. Adults with Down syndrome may live about 60 years, but this can vary.

Causes

When a baby is conceived, a normal egg cell and normal sperm cell start with 46 chromosomes. The egg and sperm cells then divide in half, each having 23 chromosomes. When a sperm with 23 chromosomes fertilizes an egg with 23 chromosomes, the baby will have a complete set of 46 chromosomes.

Sometimes an error occurs, and an egg or sperm cell may keep both copies of chromosome 21, instead of just 1 copy. This causes trisomy 21. In rare cases, an extra number 21 chromosome is attached to another chromosome, causing translocation Down syndrome. Mosaic trisomy 21 occurs when the error happens after fertilization, resulting in some normal and some abnormal cells.

Risk Factors

A mother’s age at her child’s birth is the primary factor linked to the risk of having a baby with Down syndrome. This risk increases with age, especially after 35. However, most babies with Down syndrome are born to women younger than 35 due to higher birth rates in this group.

Symptoms

  • Eyes that slant upward
  • Small ears that may fold over slightly at the top
  • Small mouth that makes the tongue appear large
  • Small nose with a flattened bridge
  • Short neck
  • Small hands with short fingers
  • Two palm creases, including one across the palm and one around the thumb base
  • Short height
  • Loose joints

Additional issues may include heart defects, intestinal problems, vision and hearing problems, thyroid disorders, and learning difficulties.

Diagnosis

Chromosome problems such as Down syndrome can often be diagnosed before birth through tests like amniocentesis, placental analysis, or noninvasive prenatal screening of the mother’s blood. Ultrasound may suggest Down syndrome but is not 100% accurate.

After birth, a physical exam and blood sample to check for the extra chromosome can confirm the diagnosis.

Treatment

There is no cure for Down syndrome, but treatments can address associated health problems:

  • Heart defects: About half of babies with Down syndrome have heart defects, some requiring surgery. An echocardiogram is recommended within the first 2 months of life.
  • Intestinal problems: Some require surgery to correct structural issues.
  • Vision problems: Crossed eyes, nearsightedness, and cataracts can be treated with glasses, surgery, or other interventions.
  • Hearing loss: Regular hearing tests and treatments for fluid buildup or nerve defects are essential.
  • Other health concerns: These include thyroid problems, leukemia, and frequent respiratory infections.
  • Learning problems: Early intervention and special education can help children achieve developmental milestones.

Additional Notes

While some claim high-dose vitamins can improve learning and development, no studies have proven this effective. Regular medical care, supportive therapies, and early interventions are key to managing Down syndrome effectively.

Complications

Complications of Down syndrome vary depending on the affected body organ and the severity of the problem. Issues include birth defects, learning problems, and facial features. A child with Down syndrome may also have heart defects and problems with vision and hearing. Treatment will vary depending on the body organ affected and the severity of the problem. Your child’s healthcare provider will discuss treatment options with you.

Prevention

Researchers don’t know how to prevent the chromosome errors that cause this disorder. There is no evidence that a parent can do anything to cause or prevent Down syndrome in their child.

For women who have had one child with Down syndrome, the chance of having another baby with Down syndrome depends on several factors. Age is one key factor. While the risk increases after age 35, most babies with Down syndrome are born to women younger than 35 because they have more babies overall.

Living with Down Syndrome

Children with Down syndrome can usually do most things that any young child can do, such as walking, talking, dressing themselves, and being toilet trained. However, they often reach these milestones later than other children. The timing of these developmental achievements varies from child to child.

Early intervention programs starting in infancy can help children with Down syndrome reach their potential. These programs, combined with special education, can enable children to attend school, learn to read and write, and take part in community activities. Many children can join regular classrooms with additional support.

Your child may benefit from physical, occupational, and speech therapy to aid their development. Support from healthcare providers, other families, and national Down syndrome organizations can provide valuable resources and guidance.

Special work programs exist for adults with Down syndrome, and many can hold regular jobs. More adults with the condition are living semi-independently in community group homes, where they care for themselves, engage in household tasks, develop friendships, and contribute to their communities.

Some people with Down syndrome marry. Most men with Down syndrome cannot father a child. Women with Down syndrome have a 1 in 2 chance of conceiving a child with Down syndrome, though many pregnancies result in miscarriage.

Key Points

  • Down syndrome (trisomy 21) is a genetic disorder that includes birth defects, learning problems, and distinct facial features. It may also involve heart defects and vision or hearing issues.
  • A mother’s age at the child’s birth is the main factor linked to the risk of having a baby with Down syndrome, with higher risk after age 35.
  • There is no known way to prevent the chromosome errors that cause Down syndrome.
  • Down syndrome can often be diagnosed before birth through prenatal testing. After birth, diagnosis can be confirmed with a physical exam and a blood test.
  • There is no cure, but treatment options such as therapy and education programs can help children with Down syndrome develop and thrive.
  • Physical, occupational, and speech therapy, along with early intervention, can significantly benefit children with Down syndrome.
