Bone Tumors

Children’s National Health System has a team of individuals who are highly skilled and experienced in the treatment of bone tumors, including osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma. Children’s patients have access to the most current therapies in treating pediatric cancer through Children’s Oncology Group’s protocols and other clinical trials.

In addition, the Patient and Family Support Program Cancer and Blood Disorders provides comprehensive mental health and psychosocial services and helpful resources for all patients treated in the Center for Cancer Blood Disorders, as well as patients’ parents and siblings.

Ewing Sarcoma

Ewing sarcoma is a cancer that occurs primarily in the bone or soft tissue. It most often affects children between 5 and 20 years old and accounts for about 1.8% of childhood cancers. Symptoms include:

  • Pain around the tumor site
  • Swelling and redness around the site
  • Fever
  • Weight loss and decreased appetite
  • Fatigue
  • Paralysis or incontinence if near the spine
  • Nerve compression symptoms like numbness or tingling

Causes for Ewing Sarcoma

Ewing sarcoma is often linked to a chromosome rearrangement between chromosomes #11 and #22. This causes a gene fusion referred to as a fusion transcript. Trauma at the tumor site may bring the condition to attention but is not considered a cause.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis may include:

  • X-rays
  • Bone scans
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • CT scans
  • Blood tests
  • Biopsy of the tumor
  • Bone marrow aspiration

Osteogenic Sarcoma

Also known as osteosarcoma, this is the most common type of bone cancer in children, often occurring between 10 and 25 years of age. Symptoms include:

  • Pain at the tumor site
  • Swelling and redness
  • Increased pain with activity
  • Limping
  • Decreased movement of the affected limb

Causes for Osteogenic Sarcoma

Causes may include:

  • Repeated trauma to the bone
  • Genetic factors (e.g., retinoblastoma, Paget's disease)
  • Exposure to ionizing radiation

Ewing Sarcoma Diagnosis

In addition to a complete medical history and physical examination of your child, diagnostic procedures for Ewing sarcoma may include:

  • X-rays: Use invisible electromagnetic energy beams to produce images of internal tissues, bones, and organs onto film. This test is used to measure and evaluate the curve.
  • Bone scans: A nuclear imaging method that evaluates any degenerative and/or arthritic changes in the joints, detects bone diseases and tumors, determines the cause of bone pain or inflammation, and rules out infections or fractures.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): Uses large magnets, radiofrequencies, and a computer to produce detailed images of organs and structures within the body. This test helps rule out associated abnormalities of the spinal cord and nerves.
  • Computer-assisted tomography scan (CT or CAT scan): Combines x-rays and computer technology to produce cross-sectional images (slices) of the body, including bones, muscles, fat, and organs. CT scans are more detailed than general x-rays.
  • Complete blood count (CBC): Measures the size, number, and maturity of different blood cells in a specific volume of blood.
  • Blood tests: Including blood chemistries to assess overall health.
  • Biopsy of the tumor: A procedure to examine the tumor's internal structure for abnormalities.
  • Bone marrow aspiration and/or biopsy: Involves taking a small amount of bone marrow fluid or solid tissue to examine for the number, size, and maturity of blood cells and/or abnormal cells.

Treatment

The specialists at Children’s National Health System aim not only to cure the cancer but also to minimize the side effects of treatment. Specific treatment for a bone tumor will be determined based on:

  • The child’s age, overall health, and medical history
  • The type, location, and size of the tumor
  • The extent of the disease

Treatment options may include:

  • Surgery
  • Cyberknife therapy
  • Radiation therapy
  • Amputation
  • Resections for metastases, such as pulmonary resections of cancer cells in the lung
  • Rehabilitation including physical and occupational therapy, and psychosocial adaptation
  • Prosthesis fitting and training
  • Antibiotics to prevent and treat infections
  • Continual follow-up care to determine response to treatment, detect recurrent disease, and manage late effects of treatment

Long-term Outlook

The prognosis and long-term survival of children with Ewing sarcoma or osteosarcoma depend on various factors:

  • The child’s age
  • The extent of the disease (presence or absence of metastasis)
  • Size and location of the tumor
  • Tumor’s response to therapy
  • The child’s tolerance of specific medications, procedures, or therapies

Every child is unique, and treatment and prognosis are tailored to their needs. Prompt medical attention and aggressive therapy are essential for the best prognosis. Continuous follow-up care is crucial to manage late effects of radiation and chemotherapy, as well as to monitor for second malignancies. Researchers continue to discover new methods to improve treatment outcomes and reduce side effects.
