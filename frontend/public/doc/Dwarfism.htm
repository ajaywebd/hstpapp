Dwarfism

Dwarfism is short stature resulting from a genetic or medical condition. It is generally defined as an adult height of 4 feet 10 inches (147 centimeters) or less. The average adult height among people with dwarfism is 4 feet (122 cm).

Many different medical conditions cause dwarfism. These disorders are divided into two broad categories:

  • Disproportionate Dwarfism: Body size is disproportionate when some parts of the body are smaller, and others are of average or above-average size. Disorders causing disproportionate dwarfism inhibit bone development.
  • Proportionate Dwarfism: A body is proportionately small if all parts are equally small and proportioned like an average-sized body. Medical conditions present at birth or appearing in early childhood limit overall growth and development.

Some people prefer the term "short stature" instead of "dwarf" or "dwarfism." It's essential to respect the preference of someone with this disorder. Short stature disorders do not include familial short stature, which is considered a normal variation with typical bone development.

Symptoms

Signs and symptoms, aside from short stature, vary significantly across the spectrum of disorders.

  • Disproportionate Dwarfism: Most people with dwarfism have disorders causing disproportionately short stature. Usually, this means a person has an average-size trunk with very short limbs. The head may also be disproportionately large compared to the body.

Almost all people with disproportionate dwarfism have normal intellectual capacities. Rare exceptions may occur due to secondary factors, such as hydrocephalus (excess fluid around the brain).

The most common cause of dwarfism is a disorder called achondroplasia, which results in the following:

  • An average-size trunk
  • Short arms and legs, particularly short upper arms and upper legs
  • Short fingers, often with a wide separation between the middle and ring fingers
  • Limited mobility at the elbows
  • A disproportionately large head with a prominent forehead and a flattened nose bridge
  • Progressive development of bowed legs
  • Progressive development of a swayed lower back
  • An adult height of about 4 feet (122 cm)

Another cause of disproportionate dwarfism is a rare disorder called spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia congenita (SEDC). Signs may include:

  • A very short trunk
  • A short neck
  • Shortened arms and legs
  • Average-size hands and feet
  • A broad, rounded chest
  • Slightly flattened cheekbones
  • Opening in the roof of the mouth (cleft palate)
  • Hip deformities causing thighbones to turn inward
  • A foot twisted or out of shape
  • Instability of the neck bones
  • Progressive hunching of the upper spine
  • Progressive development of a swayed lower back
  • Vision and hearing problems
  • Arthritis and joint movement issues
  • An adult height ranging from 3 feet (91 cm) to just over 4 feet (122 cm)

Proportionate Dwarfism results from medical conditions present at birth or appearing in early childhood that limit overall growth. In this case, the head, trunk, and limbs are all small but proportionate. These disorders may affect the development of one or more body systems.

Growth hormone deficiency is a common cause of proportionate dwarfism. It occurs when the pituitary gland does not produce enough growth hormone. Signs include:

  • Height below the third percentile on standard pediatric growth charts
  • Slower growth rate than expected for age
  • Delayed or absent sexual development during the teen years

When to see a doctor: Signs of disproportionate dwarfism are often present at birth or early infancy. Proportionate dwarfism may not be apparent immediately. See your child's doctor if you have concerns about your child's growth or development.

Causes

Most dwarfism-related conditions are genetic disorders. Many cases result from a random genetic mutation in the father's sperm or the mother's egg. However, the causes of some conditions are unknown. Examples of causes include:

  • Achondroplasia: About 80% of people with achondroplasia are born to parents of average height. A person with achondroplasia may pass a mutated or normal copy of the gene to their children.
  • Turner Syndrome: This condition affects girls and women, resulting from a missing or partially missing X chromosome.
  • Growth Hormone Deficiency: This may result from a genetic mutation or injury, but for most people with the condition, the cause is unknown.
  • Other Causes: These include other genetic disorders, hormone deficiencies, or poor nutrition. Sometimes the cause is unknown.

Complications

Complications of dwarfism-related disorders can vary, but some common issues include:

  • Disproportionate Dwarfism: Common problems include delays in motor skill development, frequent ear infections, bowing of the legs, sleep apnea, pressure on the spinal cord, hydrocephalus, crowded teeth, and progressive hunching or swaying of the back.
  • Proportionate Dwarfism: These issues often result in poorly developed organs, such as heart problems, which may significantly affect health.
  • Pregnancy: Women with disproportionate dwarfism may develop respiratory problems during pregnancy, and a C-section is often necessary.

Public Perceptions

People with dwarfism prefer not to be labeled by their condition, although some refer to themselves as dwarfs or little people. The term "midget" is considered offensive. Misconceptions about dwarfism can affect self-esteem and opportunities for success. Children with dwarfism may feel isolated and are vulnerable to teasing.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis involves measurements of height, weight, and head circumference, appearance, imaging technology, genetic tests, family history, and hormone tests.

Health Care Team

A variety of specialists may be involved in diagnosis and treatment, including endocrinologists, ENT specialists, orthopedists, geneticists, cardiologists, ophthalmologists, psychologists, neurologists, orthodontists, developmental therapists, and occupational therapists.

Treatment

Treatment for dwarfism usually addresses complications rather than increasing stature. This may include surgical treatments, limb lengthening, hormone therapy, and ongoing healthcare.

Lifestyle and Home Remedies

At-home care may include using proper infant car seats, supporting the child's head and neck, monitoring for complications, encouraging good posture, maintaining a healthy diet, and promoting healthy activities like swimming or cycling while avoiding collision sports.

Coping and Support

Steps to help children cope with dwarfism include seeking help from organizations like Little People of America, modifying the home, using adaptive tools, educating school personnel, and addressing teasing.
