Concussions

A concussion is a type of brain injury caused by a serious hit to the head. It can temporarily alter the way the brain works. The skull and spinal fluid typically protect the brain, but a hard enough impact can cause the brain to shift and hit the inside of the skull, resulting in a concussion.

Signs of a Concussion

Concussions can be challenging to identify without medical advice. Here are some signs that may indicate a concussion:

  • Loss of consciousness
  • Severe or worsening headache
  • Blurred vision
  • Trouble walking
  • Confusion or nonsensical speech
  • Slurred speech
  • Unresponsiveness

Parents should call a doctor immediately if the child experiences vomiting, dizziness, headache, or trouble concentrating after a head injury.

Two Important Steps to Take

If a coach or parent suspects a concussion, follow these two steps:

  • Stop playing or practicing immediately.
  • Consult a doctor before returning to play or regular activities.

Concussions can happen during sports, bike accidents, or falls. It’s crucial to get medical clearance before resuming normal activities.

Protect Your Brain

Kids who rush recovery after a concussion risk further injury because they are still wobbly from the first concussion. Incomplete recovery can lead to prolonged headaches and difficulty focusing on tasks like studying. Your brain is the "boss" of your body — it helps you walk, talk, and think, so protecting it is crucial!

Want to be a concussion fighter? Here's how:

  • Tell your coach or parent immediately if you hit your head.
  • Wear a seatbelt when traveling in a car.
  • Always wear a helmet during activities like biking, football, hockey, or skateboarding.

While helmets can't prevent a concussion entirely, they can reduce the risk of more severe brain injuries. The good news is that most kids recover fully after a concussion and can return to normal activities like school and sports.

Treatment

The best treatment for a concussion is complete rest from physical and mental activity. Key steps include:

  • Limiting exposure to loud music, computers, and TVs if they worsen symptoms.
  • Adjusting schoolwork, postponing tests and projects, and excusing students from gym or recess.
  • Monitoring children for worsening symptoms or behavioral changes, such as grogginess or agitation, and reporting them to a doctor immediately.

Prevention

Not all concussions can be prevented, but many can be avoided by following these precautions:

  • Wear helmets for activities like biking, skateboarding, or playing contact sports (football, hockey, lacrosse).
  • Ensure helmets fit properly and are in good condition.
  • Athletes should use safe playing techniques and follow game rules.
  • Encourage athletes to report head injuries to their coach, trainer, or parent, even if it means stopping play.

Never ignore a head injury, no matter how minor it seems. Taking these precautions helps protect your brain and ensures a full recovery if an injury does occur.
