Febrile Seizure

A febrile seizure is a convulsion in a child caused by a spike in body temperature, often due to an infection. It occurs in young children with normal development and no history of neurological symptoms. Though frightening, febrile seizures are usually harmless and do not indicate serious health issues.

Symptoms

A febrile seizure typically involves shaking all over and a loss of consciousness. In some cases, stiffness or twitching may occur in one area of the body.

  • Fever higher than 100.4°F (38.0°C)
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Shaking or jerking of arms and legs

Febrile seizures are classified as:

  • Simple Febrile Seizures: Lasts a few seconds to 15 minutes, does not recur within 24 hours, and is not specific to one body part.
  • Complex Febrile Seizures: Lasts longer than 15 minutes, occurs more than once within 24 hours, or is confined to one side of the body.

These seizures often occur within 24 hours of fever onset and can be the first sign of illness.

When to See a Doctor

  • Visit your doctor after your child's first febrile seizure, even if it lasts only a few seconds.
  • Call emergency services if the seizure lasts longer than five minutes or is accompanied by:
    • Vomiting
    • A stiff neck
    • Breathing problems
    • Extreme sleepiness

Causes

Febrile seizures are usually caused by elevated body temperature. Common triggers include:

  • Infections: Viral infections like influenza and roseola often cause high fevers, leading to febrile seizures.
  • Post-Immunization Seizures: Fevers following vaccinations such as DTaP or MMR may trigger seizures. The fever—not the vaccine—causes the seizure.

Risk Factors

  • Young Age: Most febrile seizures occur between 6 months and 5 years, with the highest risk between 12–18 months.
  • Family History: A family tendency to have seizures with fever can increase susceptibility.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis includes reviewing the child's medical history and identifying the fever's cause. Tests may include:

  • Simple Febrile Seizures: Typically diagnosed based on history in vaccinated children without other risk factors.
  • Complex Febrile Seizures: May require additional tests like:
    • Blood and urine tests to check for infection
    • Spinal tap (lumbar puncture) for central nervous system infections
    • EEG to measure brain activity
    • MRI for unusual head size, neurological abnormalities, or prolonged seizures

Treatment

Most febrile seizures stop on their own within a couple of minutes. If your child has a febrile seizure, stay calm and follow these steps:

  • Place your child on his or her side on a surface where they won't fall.
  • Start timing the seizure.
  • Stay close to watch and comfort your child.
  • Remove hard or sharp objects near your child.
  • Loosen tight or restrictive clothing.
  • Do not restrain your child or interfere with their movements.
  • Do not put anything in your child’s mouth.

If the seizure lasts more than five minutes or if your child has repeated seizures, call emergency medical services immediately.

For more serious episodes, a doctor may order medication to stop prolonged seizures. Your child may need hospitalization for observation if:

  • The seizure is prolonged.
  • The seizure is accompanied by a serious infection.
  • The source of the infection cannot be identified.

Hospitalization is usually unnecessary for simple febrile seizures.

Complications

Most febrile seizures cause no lasting effects. Simple febrile seizures do not result in brain damage, intellectual disability, or learning disabilities, and they do not indicate a more serious underlying disorder.

Febrile seizures are provoked and do not indicate epilepsy, which involves unprovoked seizures caused by abnormal brain activity.

Recurrent Febrile Seizures

The most common complication is the recurrence of febrile seizures. The risk is higher if:

  • The first seizure occurred with a low fever.
  • The period between the fever's onset and the seizure was short.
  • A family member has a history of febrile seizures.
  • Your child was younger than 18 months at the time of the first seizure.

Prevention

Most febrile seizures occur in the first few hours of a fever during the initial rise in body temperature.

Medications for Comfort

Giving your child infants' or children's acetaminophen (e.g., Tylenol) or ibuprofen (e.g., Motrin) at the beginning of a fever may make them more comfortable, but it will not prevent seizures. Avoid giving aspirin to children recovering from chickenpox or flu-like symptoms, as it may lead to Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition.

Prescription Prevention Medications

Rarely, prescription anticonvulsant medications are used to prevent febrile seizures, but their side effects may outweigh their benefits. Rectal diazepam (Diastat) or nasal midazolam might be prescribed for children prone to long seizures or multiple seizures within 24 hours. These medications are used as needed to treat seizures lasting longer than five minutes, not to prevent them.
