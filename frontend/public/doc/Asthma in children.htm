Asthma in Children

In childhood asthma, the lungs and airways become easily inflamed when exposed to certain triggers, such as inhaling airborne pollen or catching a cold or another respiratory infection. Childhood asthma can cause bothersome daily symptoms that interfere with play, sports, school, and sleep. In some children, unmanaged asthma can cause dangerous asthma attacks.

Childhood asthma isn't a different disease from asthma in adults, but children face unique challenges. It is a leading cause of emergency department visits, hospitalizations, and missed school days. Although childhood asthma can't be cured and may continue into adulthood, the right treatment can help keep symptoms under control and prevent damage to growing lungs.

Symptoms

Common childhood asthma signs and symptoms include:

  • Frequent, intermittent coughing
  • A whistling or wheezing sound when exhaling
  • Shortness of breath
  • Chest congestion or tightness
  • Chest pain, particularly in younger children

Other signs and symptoms include:

  • Trouble sleeping due to shortness of breath, coughing, or wheezing
  • Bouts of coughing or wheezing that worsen with respiratory infections like colds or flu
  • Delayed recovery or bronchitis after respiratory infections
  • Difficulty breathing that may limit play or exercise
  • Fatigue, potentially from poor sleep

The first signs of asthma in young children may be recurrent wheezing triggered by a respiratory virus. Asthma linked with respiratory allergies becomes more common as children grow older. Symptoms vary from child to child and may improve or worsen over time.

It may be challenging to determine if your child's symptoms are due to asthma or another condition, as periodic or persistent wheezing and similar symptoms can be caused by infectious bronchitis or other respiratory problems.

When to See a Doctor

Take your child to see a doctor as soon as possible if you suspect asthma. Early treatment can help control day-to-day symptoms and may prevent asthma attacks.

Make an appointment if you notice:

  • Constant or intermittent coughing, or coughing linked to physical activity
  • Wheezing or whistling sounds when exhaling
  • Shortness of breath or rapid breathing
  • Complaints of chest tightness
  • Repeated episodes of suspected bronchitis or pneumonia

Children with asthma might say things like, "My chest feels funny" or "I'm always coughing." Listen for nighttime coughing, which may or may not wake your child. Strong emotions, such as crying or laughing, can also trigger coughing or wheezing.

If your child is diagnosed with asthma, creating an asthma action plan can help you and other caregivers monitor symptoms and know what to do if an asthma attack occurs.

Causes

The underlying causes of childhood asthma aren't fully understood. Generally, an overly sensitive immune system plays a role. Some factors thought to be involved include:

  • Inherited traits
  • Certain types of airway infections at a very young age
  • Exposure to environmental factors, such as cigarette smoke or other air pollution

Increased immune system sensitivity causes the lungs and airways to swell and produce mucus in response to certain triggers. Reaction to a trigger may be delayed, making it difficult to identify. Triggers vary from child to child and can include:

  • Viral infections like the common cold
  • Exposure to air pollutants, such as tobacco smoke
  • Allergies to dust mites, pet dander, pollen, or mold
  • Physical activity
  • Weather changes or cold air

Sometimes, asthma symptoms occur without any apparent triggers.

Risk Factors

Factors that may increase your child's likelihood of developing asthma include:

  • Exposure to tobacco smoke
  • Previous allergic reactions, including skin reactions, food allergies, or hay fever (allergic rhinitis)
  • A family history of asthma, allergic rhinitis, hives, or eczema
  • Living in an urban area with increased exposure to air pollution
  • Obesity
  • Respiratory conditions, such as chronic rhinitis, sinusitis, or pneumonia
  • Heartburn (gastroesophageal reflux disease, or GERD)
  • Being male
  • Being black

Complications

Asthma may cause a number of complications, including:

  • Severe asthma attacks requiring emergency treatment or hospital care
  • Permanent narrowing of the airways (bronchial tubes)
  • Missed school days or getting behind in school
  • Poor sleep and fatigue
  • Symptoms that interfere with play, sports, or other activities

Prevention

Careful planning and avoiding asthma triggers are the best ways to prevent asthma attacks:

  • Limit exposure to asthma triggers by helping your child avoid allergens and irritants that trigger asthma symptoms.
  • Avoid smoking around your child. Tobacco smoke exposure during infancy is a strong risk factor for childhood asthma.
  • Encourage your child to stay active. Controlled asthma allows for regular physical activity, which can improve lung function.

  • Help your child maintain a healthy weight, as obesity can worsen asthma symptoms and lead to other health issues.
  • Keep heartburn under control, as acid reflux can worsen asthma symptoms.

Diagnosis

Asthma can be hard to diagnose. The doctor will consider symptom frequency and may use tests to rule out other conditions and determine the cause.

Conditions with asthma-like symptoms:

  • Rhinitis
  • Sinusitis
  • Acid reflux (GERD)
  • Airway abnormalities
  • Vocal cord dysfunction
  • Respiratory tract infections, such as bronchiolitis and RSV

Diagnosis may involve lung function tests for children over age 5, measuring how quickly and how much air is exhaled. For younger children, diagnosis may rely on observing symptoms over time.

Treatment

The goal of asthma treatment is to keep symptoms under control. Effective treatment allows:

  • Minimal or no symptoms
  • Few or no asthma flare-ups
  • Normal physical activities
  • Minimal use of quick-relief inhalers
  • Few or no medication side effects

Treatment involves both preventive measures and managing flare-ups. The right medication depends on age, symptoms, and triggers.

Long-term control medications include:

  • Inhaled corticosteroids: Medications like fluticasone (Flovent), budesonide (Pulmicort), and mometasone (Asmanex) reduce inflammation. Benefits may take days to weeks, with minor growth impact in children.
  • Leukotriene modifiers: Oral medications such as montelukast (Singulair) and zileuton (Zyflo) help prevent symptoms for up to 24 hours. Rarely, these may cause psychological side effects.
  • Combination inhalers: These include corticosteroids and long-acting beta agonists (LABA), such as Advair and Symbicort. LABA medications should always be combined with corticosteroids for safety.
  • Theophylline: A daily pill that helps relax muscles around the airways. Although less common, itâ€™s still used for maintaining open airways.

Quick-Relief Medications

Quick-relief medications quickly open swollen airways that limit breathing. Also called rescue medications, they are used as needed for rapid, short-term symptom relief during an asthma attack â€” or before exercise if your child's doctor recommends it.

Types of quick-relief medications include:

  • Short-acting beta agonists: Inhaled bronchodilator medications, such as albuterol (ProAir HFA, Ventolin HFA) and levalbuterol (Xopenex HFA), act within minutes and last several hours to ease symptoms during an asthma attack.
  • Ipratropium (Atrovent HFA): Prescribed for immediate symptom relief, this bronchodilator relaxes the airways, making it easier to breathe. It's mainly used for emphysema and chronic bronchitis but can treat asthma attacks.
  • Oral and intravenous corticosteroids: Medications like prednisone and methylprednisolone relieve airway inflammation from severe asthma, used short-term due to potential long-term side effects.
  • Immunomodulatory agents: Mepolizumab (Nucala) may be suitable for children with severe eosinophilic asthma.

Treatment for Allergy-Induced Asthma

If your child's asthma is triggered or worsened by allergies, allergy treatment may help. Options include:

  • Omalizumab (Xolair): For people with allergies and severe asthma, this medication reduces the immune response to allergens like pollen, dust mites, and pet dander. Administered by injection every two to four weeks.
  • Allergy medications: Includes oral and nasal antihistamines, decongestants, and nasal sprays (corticosteroid, cromolyn, and ipratropium).
  • Allergy shots (immunotherapy): Given weekly for a few months, then monthly for 3-5 years, gradually reducing the immune system's response to specific allergens.

Inhaled Medication Devices

Inhaled short- and long-term control medications are used by inhaling a measured dose:

  • Older children and teens may use a handheld device like a pressurized metered dose inhaler or an inhaler that releases a fine powder.
  • Infants and toddlers need a face mask attached to a metered dose inhaler or nebulizer to ensure correct dosing.
  • Babies use a nebulizer, which turns liquid medication into droplets. The baby wears a face mask and breathes normally while receiving medication.

Steps to Reduce Exposure to Triggers

Reducing exposure to asthma triggers can lessen the likelihood of asthma attacks. Steps include:

  • Maintain low humidity at home: In damp climates, consider a dehumidifier.
  • Keep indoor air clean: Have a professional check your HVAC system annually, change filters regularly, and consider a small-particle filter.
  • Reduce pet dander: Avoid pets with fur or feathers if your child is allergic. Bathing or grooming pets regularly can also help.
  • Use your air conditioner: Reduces airborne pollen and dust mites, while lowering indoor humidity. If no air conditioning, keep windows closed during pollen season.
  • Minimize dust: Use dust-proof covers for bedding, replace carpeting with hard flooring, and use washable curtains and blinds.
  • Clean regularly: Clean your home weekly to remove dust and allergens.
  • Reduce exposure to cold air: If cold air worsens asthma, wearing a face mask outside can help.
