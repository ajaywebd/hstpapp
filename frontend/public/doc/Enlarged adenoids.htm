Enlarged Adenoids

Adenoids are small patches of tissue located at the back of the throat, similar to the tonsils but positioned right above them. While tonsils are visible at the back of the throat, adenoids are not directly visible. Both are part of the immune system, helping to prevent and fight infections. Enlarged adenoids can cause various problems, but they are not essential and can often be treated by removal.

Causes

Adenoids are present at birth and grow until a child is between the ages of 3 and 5. Normally, they begin to shrink after age 7 and significantly in adulthood. Enlarged adenoids can occur due to:

  • Infections causing temporary enlargement
  • Allergies
  • Enlargement from birth

Although they usually shrink after an infection subsides, in some cases, they remain enlarged.

Symptoms

Enlarged adenoids can cause several symptoms, including:

  • Blocked or stuffy nose
  • Ear problems
  • Snoring
  • Sore throat
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Swollen neck glands
  • Breathing problems through the nose
  • Sleep apnea
  • Glue ear or fluid buildup in the middle ear
  • Cracked lips and dry mouth

Diagnosis

Diagnosis involves:

  • Physical examination and symptom review
  • Using a mirror or flexible endoscope to view the adenoids
  • Blood tests to check for infections
  • X-rays in certain cases
  • Sleep studies for severe cases with potential sleep apnea

Treatment

Treatment depends on the severity of the condition:

  • Observation: If the adenoids are not causing severe problems, the doctor may wait to see if they shrink naturally.
  • Medication: Nasal steroids may be prescribed to reduce size and inflammation.
  • Surgery: An adenoidectomy may be performed if problems persist despite medication. If frequent tonsil infections occur, the tonsils may also be removed. The surgery involves:
    • General anesthesia
    • Lasting under two hours
    • Post-surgery care, including antibiotics and mild pain relievers

Post-surgery symptoms may include a sore throat, minor bleeding, earaches, or a blocked nose, which usually resolve within a few weeks.

Long-Term Outlook

Enlarged adenoids are common and very treatable. Early examination and treatment can prevent complications. In mild cases, simple antibiotics may suffice, while more severe cases may require surgery.
